There is a version of you that never wastes time. Never takes a detour. Never keeps a backup plan because the first plan always works. Every calorie is used, every hour is billed, every resource is optimized to its absolute limit. This version of you sounds admirable. It is also, in a very precise biological sense, already dead.

Evolution is the longest-running performance review in history — 3.8 billion years of iterating, discarding, and refining. And what did it conclude? Not that perfection wins. That slack wins. That redundancy wins. That the organism with a little waste built in, with organs that do more than strictly necessary, with immune responses that occasionally overreact — that organism survives. The perfectly efficient one gets wiped out the first time conditions change in a way nobody predicted.

Morgan Housel makes this point quietly but devastatingly in Same as Ever. He calls it the casualties of perfection. The idea is simple: the drive to eliminate all inefficiency, to cut every redundancy, to squeeze every last drop of productivity out of a system — that drive, taken far enough, will kill you. Or your company. Or your relationship. Or your ability to think clearly under pressure.

Consider what biologists call "redundant capacity." You have two kidneys, but you only need one to survive. You have more lung tissue than you typically use. Your liver can regenerate from a fraction of itself. None of this is efficient. None of it would survive a consultant's cost-cutting review. And all of it is what lets you survive a car accident, a cancer diagnosis, a brutal flu. The waste is not a flaw in the design. The waste is the design.

The same logic runs through every system that has to survive contact with reality. A bridge is built to hold far more weight than any engineer expects it to carry. A hospital keeps beds empty that it hopes never to fill. An army maintains supply chains that look bloated in peacetime precisely so they can function in wartime. The redundancy is not evidence of poor planning. It is the plan.

Now apply this to a life, and suddenly things get uncomfortable.

We have built a culture that treats busyness as virtue and slack as failure. The person who has "nothing to do on Saturday" is someone who has not optimized well enough. The person who keeps a savings account that earns almost no interest — when that money could be "working" — is naive about compounding. The person who maintains friendships with no immediate practical utility is wasting social capital. Every moment that is not producing should be converted into a moment that is.

This is optimization logic, and it is seductive because it is often correct in the short run. Cut the fat, and you do become faster. Eliminate the buffer, and the numbers do look better. Say yes to every opportunity, and your calendar does fill with impressive things.

But Housel points to what happens next. A single unexpected shock — a bad quarter, a health scare, a relationship that quietly needed more of you than you gave it — and the perfectly optimized system has no room to absorb it. The bridge that was built to hold exactly what was expected collapses the first time a truck is slightly overloaded. The company that eliminated all inventory buffers in the name of efficiency discovers, during a supply chain disruption, that efficiency is a fair-weather virtue.

Nassim Taleb has a word for systems that gain from disorder: antifragile. But before you can be antifragile, you have to survive being fragile. Optimization removes the slack that makes survival possible in the first place.

There is a more personal version of this that is harder to admit. Think about the parts of your life that look like waste. The long walk you take with no destination. The friendship you maintain even though you haven't seen that person in two years. The hour of reading you protect even when the inbox is overflowing. The project you work on just because it interests you, with no plan to monetize it. By pure productivity logic, these are inefficiencies to be eliminated. By survival logic, these are exactly what keeps you from breaking.

Researcher Sherry Turkle at MIT has spent decades studying what happens to human beings when they remove all unstructured time in favor of productive connection. The finding is not flattering: they become less creative, less resilient, and curiously less effective even at the productive tasks they were optimizing for. The mind, it turns out, requires what looks like waste — daydreaming, wandering, boredom — to consolidate what it has learned and generate what it has not yet thought of.

The same holds at the level of organizations. Jim Collins, in his research for Good to Great, found that the companies that sustained excellence for decades were not the ones with the tightest operations. They were the ones that maintained what he called "productive capacity" — extra people, extra time, extra capital that could be redirected when the unexpected arrived. The perfectly lean companies looked better in the short-term comparisons. They did not last.

Housel's insight is not that you should be careless or lazy. It is that there is a version of discipline that destroys itself — that the relentless elimination of everything that doesn't immediately contribute is, paradoxically, what makes you most vulnerable when it matters most. The person who has never built any slack into their schedule has no capacity to help a friend in crisis, to think clearly about a hard decision, or to simply recover from being human.

The deepest version of this idea is about identity. When you optimize every corner of your life, you eventually become a person with no margin — no curiosity that doesn't serve a goal, no relationship that doesn't serve a function, no day that doesn't serve a plan. You become efficient. You also become brittle. And brittle things do not survive the part of life that nobody schedules: the diagnosis, the loss, the moment when everything you were optimizing for turns out to matter less than you thought.

Evolution's answer was not perfection. Its answer was robust imperfection — organisms that carry more capacity than today demands, precisely because tomorrow is unpredictable. That is not a romantic idea. It is a 3.8-billion-year empirical result.

The question is not whether you can afford the slack. It is whether you can afford not to have it.

📌 Takeaway: This week, identify one thing in your life you've been optimizing toward zero — an empty evening, a low-stakes friendship, an unscheduled hour — and instead of filling it, protect it deliberately. That margin is not waste. It is your reserve capacity.

💡 Extension: Nassim Taleb in Antifragile argues the opposite of fragile is not robust but antifragile — systems that actually gain from stress and disorder. Housel's point and Taleb's point are complementary: first you need enough slack to survive shocks (Housel), then you can build systems that benefit from them (Taleb). Arthur C. Brooks in From Strength to Strength touches a related note — the professionals who burned out fastest were those who had eliminated every non-productive pursuit from their lives, leaving nothing to fall back on when their primary identity cracked.
