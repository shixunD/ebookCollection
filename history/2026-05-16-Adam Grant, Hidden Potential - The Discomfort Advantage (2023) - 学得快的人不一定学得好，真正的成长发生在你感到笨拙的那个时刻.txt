The Discomfort Advantage
Based on Adam Grant, Hidden Potential (2023)

Here is a question worth sitting with: When was the last time you felt genuinely awkward while learning something?

Not mildly challenged. Not pleasantly stretched. Genuinely uncomfortable — the kind of discomfort where you feel slow, clumsy, maybe even a little embarrassed. If you cannot remember, that might be the most important thing you read today.

We tend to measure the quality of our learning by how smooth it feels. When a lesson flows, when we absorb information quickly, when a new skill clicks into place — we call that good learning. But a growing body of research in cognitive science suggests we have this exactly backwards. The feeling of fluency is often an illusion. And the moments that feel the most like failure are frequently the ones where the most real growth is happening.

Adam Grant calls this the discomfort advantage — and it sits at the center of his book Hidden Potential.


The Fluency Trap

For decades, education systems around the world have been organized around a seductive idea: that students learn best when instruction is matched to their preferred learning style. Visual learners need diagrams. Auditory learners need lectures. Kinesthetic learners need hands-on experience. Parents loved it. Teachers loved it. It felt respectful of individual differences.

There is just one problem. When researchers conducted a comprehensive review of decades of studies on learning styles, they found no adequate evidence base to justify the practice. Students did not perform better when their instruction matched their stated preferences. In controlled experiments and longitudinal studies alike, the theory simply did not hold up.

What the research did find is something more counterintuitive: sometimes you learn better in the mode that makes you the most uncomfortable, precisely because the friction forces your brain to work harder.

Psychologists call this desirable difficulty (required difficulty) — the principle that introducing obstacles into the learning process can actually accelerate long-term retention and transfer. Retrieving information from memory is harder than re-reading it, but the retrieval process itself deepens the neural trace. Being asked to teach something before you feel ready is uncomfortable, but that discomfort forces you to identify the gaps in your understanding.

The fluency you feel when re-reading your notes is not learning. It is recognition. And recognition is cheap.


Steve Martin Did Not Have the Gene

Grant opens this idea with a story about Steve Martin. When Martin first started doing stand-up in the 1960s, he bombed — repeatedly. A heckler once stood up mid-show and threw a glass of red wine at him. One critic called him the most serious booking error in the history of Los Angeles.

Martin was learning through the methods that felt natural to him: listening to other comedians, watching their mannerisms, borrowing material, improvising onstage. It felt like learning. It had the texture of progress. But the results were lackluster.

The method that Martin had completely written off was writing. He hated it. It did not come naturally to him. It was, in his own words, hard, so hard.

Eventually, facing a wall in his career, he forced himself to write his own material. It was agonizing at first. His writer's block was so severe that on his first professional writing assignment, he called his roommate to borrow a joke. But something slow and durable began to happen. Writing forced him to strip his humor to its bare bones. It eliminated the rambling that had plagued his live performances. It taught him the architecture of a punch line in a way that improvisation never could.

By the mid-1970s, Martin was selling out arenas. And the writing he had avoided became not only a tool for comedy but the foundation of his acting career.

The lesson Grant draws from this is precise: the problem was not that Martin lacked talent for writing. The problem was that he had confused his preference with his optimal method. He had been playing only to his comfort zone, and comfort zones, by definition, are where growth slows down.


The Polyglot Secret

Grant extends this insight to language learning through the stories of Sara Maria Hasbun and Benny Lewis — two people who became fluent in multiple languages as adults, long after the so-called critical window for language acquisition had supposedly closed.

Both of them, at first, assumed they simply lacked the language gene. Sara Maria had studied Spanish for six years and remained essentially monolingual. Benny spent eleven years on Irish and five on German and could barely hold a conversation in either.

What changed was not their ability. What changed was their willingness to speak before they felt ready.

In school, language learning is mostly passive: you read vocabulary lists, memorize grammar rules, study textbooks. You get to stay comfortable. The moment of actual speaking — the moment of potential embarrassment, of mispronunciation, of sounding like a child — is deferred indefinitely.

Hasbun and Lewis stopped deferring. They stepped into the arena before they felt ready, accepted the discomfort of sounding foolish, and discovered that competence grows in the doing of it — not in the studying toward it.

Grant summarizes this with a principle that is simple and hard: put yourself in the ring before you feel ready. You do not need to get comfortable before you practice your skills. Your comfort grows as you practice your skills.


What This Means for How We Teach and How We Grow

There is a structural implication here that goes beyond personal development. If we design schools and workplaces to maximize the feeling of smoothness — to remove friction, to match instruction to preference, to protect people from the discomfort of difficulty — we may be engineering environments that feel productive but are quietly undermining the very growth we are trying to promote.

Grant points to Finland's education system as a counterexample. Finnish schools are not built around identifying gifted students and accelerating them forward. They are built around the assumption that every student is teachable, and that the obstacle in front of a struggling student is more often an instructional failure than a capacity failure. Teachers are trusted as professionals, given autonomy to adapt to individual students, and kept with the same cohort of children for multiple years to build the deep familiarity that real coaching requires.

The result is not a system that feels easy. It is a system where difficulty is redesigned — made purposeful, scaffolded, and embedded in a context of genuine care and high expectation. That is a different thing entirely from simply making learning hard for its own sake.

The insight is not that discomfort is automatically good. Pointless difficulty is just frustration. The insight is that when discomfort is chosen deliberately — when you stretch into the method that does not come naturally, when you speak the language before you are ready, when you write the material you would rather avoid — something durable happens that smooth learning cannot produce.

Angela Duckworth's research on self-control offers a useful parallel: the people with the most discipline do not white-knuckle their way through difficulty. They change the situation to make it less aversive. They find ways to make the uncomfortable task engaging — what Grant calls deliberate play (deliberate play) — so that the friction of growth becomes something you can sustain over time rather than something you endure once and then flee.


Today's action: Identify one skill or subject you are currently learning by staying in your comfort zone — the method that feels easiest, most natural. Today, try approaching it through the mode that makes you most uncomfortable. If you always listen, write. If you always read, teach out loud. If you always study, just start doing.

📌 Takeaway: The feeling of fluency is often the enemy of real learning. Growth hides in the methods you have been avoiding, not in the ones that already feel easy.

💡 Extension: Carol Dweck in Mindset argues that people with a growth mindset embrace challenges as opportunities — but Grant pushes further: it is not enough to believe difficulty is valuable, you have to actively seek out the specific kinds of discomfort your current approach is designed to avoid.
