The Hidden Competing Commitment
Inspired by Robert Kegan & Lisa Laskow Lahey, Immunity to Change (2009)

You have probably tried to change something about yourself more than once. Maybe it was a communication habit, a tendency to overcommit, a pattern of avoiding difficult conversations. You made a plan. You were sincere. And then — almost without noticing — you were back to exactly where you started.

Harvard psychologists Robert Kegan and Lisa Laskow Lahey spent decades studying this phenomenon. Their conclusion is one of the most precise and disquieting things written about human behavior in the last thirty years: you are not failing to change because you lack willpower or motivation. You are failing because you are simultaneously, and unconsciously, committed to not changing.

They call this the immunity to change — a name borrowed deliberately from biology. Your immune system does not attack you out of malice. It attacks foreign objects because protecting the existing system is its job. The same logic applies to psychological change. Every genuine desire to change activates a hidden competing commitment — a deeper, older, often unconscious belief about what will happen if you actually do change. And that hidden commitment is almost always stronger than the stated goal.

Here is how it works in practice. A senior leader says she wants to delegate more and stop micromanaging. She is sincere — the goal is written in her journal. But she keeps pulling work back. When Kegan and Lahey walked her through their diagnostic process, she uncovered the hidden layer: a deep, unexamined commitment to being seen as indispensable. If she truly delegated, her inner system feared, she might become irrelevant. The micromanaging was not a flaw. It was a protection mechanism running exactly as designed.

Or consider the person who genuinely wants stronger boundaries — and keeps breaking them. Behind the stated goal often lives a competing commitment that sounds something like: I cannot let anyone feel disappointed by me. Or: If I say no, people will stop valuing me. The boundary-setting behavior does not fail because the person is weak. It fails because the cost of succeeding, to the hidden system, feels existential.

Kegan and Lahey call the underlying belief the "big assumption" — the mental model that makes the competing commitment feel not like a choice but like a law of physics. Big assumptions tend to be invisible precisely because they feel obviously true. They are not examined because they have never needed to be examined. They just run in the background, silently vetoing every genuine effort at change.

The elegance of their framework — and what makes it so unsettling — is that it reframes failure. Every time you fail to follow through on a change goal, you have not displayed weakness. You have displayed extraordinary psychological coherence. The immune system is doing its job. The problem is that it was calibrated in a context that no longer exists, protecting you from threats that may not be real anymore.

This has direct implications for how we think about growth — in ourselves and in the people we lead or teach. Blame, pressure, and more detailed accountability systems will not break an immunity to change. They are all inputs that the immune system already knows how to handle. What actually creates movement is something more uncomfortable: honest inquiry into what you are protecting, and a direct test of whether the big assumption is actually true.

Kegan and Lahey suggest a deceptively simple diagnostic. Take a goal you have genuinely failed to sustain. Ask: What am I doing instead? What worry does that behavior relieve? What does it protect? Follow the thread until you reach a belief that feels non-negotiable — the one that, if threatened, produces a subtle but real sense of danger. That is the competing commitment. That is what you are actually optimizing for.

Once you see it clearly, you can run a small, safe experiment. Not to prove the big assumption wrong through willpower — but to test it, gently, with real evidence. What actually happens if you let someone down once? What actually happens if you are not the most prepared person in the room? Often, the catastrophe the immune system was guarding against never arrives. And that is the beginning of real change — not the kind driven by motivation, but the kind that becomes possible when the system no longer needs to protect against a threat that was never as large as it seemed.

Change is not primarily a problem of effort. It is a problem of visibility.

---

Today's action: Pick one change goal you have failed to sustain. Write down what you keep doing instead. Then ask: what is that behavior protecting me from? Follow the answer down one more level. Write the hidden commitment in one honest sentence.

📌 Takeaway: You do not fail to change because you are not trying hard enough. You fail because part of you is trying harder — to stay exactly where you are.

💡 Extension: (Brené Brown in Daring Greatly offers a related view — she argues that the armored behaviors we cannot seem to drop are almost always protecting something real: a wound, a fear, an identity that feels fragile. Kegan and Lahey give that insight a precise diagnostic structure.)
