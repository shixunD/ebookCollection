Collaborative Overload
Inspired by Adam Grant & Reb Rebele, Harvard Business Review — "Collaborative Overload" (2016)

Think of the most helpful person you know at work. The one who always says yes. The one whose calendar is a graveyard of other people's priorities. The one who, if you asked them to review something at 10pm, would actually do it.

Now ask: how are they really doing?

In 2016, Adam Grant and Reb Rebele published a study in the Harvard Business Review that reframed one of the most celebrated virtues in modern organizational culture. Their finding was precise and uncomfortable: in most organizations, 35 to 50 percent of the value created by collaboration comes from just 3 to 5 percent of employees. These are the go-to people — the generous, reliable, knowledgeable ones everyone runs to. And they are quietly burning out. Their own work performance, their creativity, their health: all of it erodes in direct proportion to how much they give.

Grant had spent years arguing — compellingly and correctly — that givers outperform takers and matchers over the long arc of a career. His research in Give and Take documented this in detail. But the HBR piece added a crucial qualifier that often gets lost in the enthusiasm for generosity: not all giving is equal. There is high-value giving, which compounds over time and builds networks and reputation. And there is depleting giving — reactive, boundaryless, driven by the inability to say no — which extracts from the giver far more than it creates for anyone.

The distinction sounds simple. It is not. Because the forces that produce collaborative overload are not character flaws. They are deeply structural.

Organizations reward visible helpfulness. Being the person others rely on generates social capital, affirmation, and a sense of purpose. Saying no feels selfish, even when it is strategically necessary. And so the most capable, most conscientious employees — the ones whose judgment and knowledge everyone values — accumulate an ever-growing shadow workload of mentoring, answering, reviewing, advising, and coordinating. This shadow workload is nearly invisible in formal performance reviews. It does not appear on project trackers. But it occupies real time, real cognitive bandwidth, and real emotional energy.

Organizational psychologist Rob Cross, whose research fed directly into Grant and Rebele's framework, tracked how collaboration time in companies had increased by more than 50 percent over the previous two decades — yet individual performance ratings had not improved proportionally. In many cases they had declined. The people drowning in requests were not contributing less. They were contributing in ways the organization could not see or measure, while their own core work suffered.

Here is the insight that stings the most: the problem is not that these people are too generous. It is that they have never been given permission — or taught how — to be strategic about their generosity. Grant's research distinguishes between what he calls "selfless giving" (saying yes to everything, with no filter) and "otherish giving" (being genuinely generous, but in ways that align with one's own energy, strengths, and longer-term capacity). The selfless giver becomes a bottleneck and eventually a cautionary tale. The otherish giver builds something sustainable.

This matters enormously in educational and leadership contexts — which is likely familiar terrain. Think about the teacher who is also the default mentor, the informal counselor, the curriculum writer, the parent liaison, and the colleague everyone vents to. Or the school leader whose door is always open, who takes every call, who attends every event. The intention is beautiful. The structural result is that their deepest, most irreplaceable work — teaching with full presence, making clear-eyed strategic decisions, modeling creative thinking — gets crowded out by an unending stream of low-cost requests.

The solution Grant and Rebele propose is not to become less generous. It is to become more intentional. Shift from availability to thoughtful engagement. Identify the requests where your input genuinely creates disproportionate value versus the ones that could be handled by someone else, or not at all. Protect enough uninterrupted time that your actual work — the work that required years to develop — can actually happen.

Generosity without strategy is not virtue. It is just a slower form of depletion. And the people who understand this earliest tend to give more over a lifetime, not less — because they are still standing.

---

Today's action: Look at your calendar or task list for this week. Identify one commitment that is genuinely low-value to others and high-cost to you. Practice declining it — or redirecting it — with one clear, kind sentence.

📌 Takeaway: The most generous thing you can do long-term is protect the capacity that makes your giving worth anything in the first place.

💡 Extension: (Adam Grant in Give and Take makes the complementary point — the highest-performing givers are not selfless, they are "otherish": they care about others' interests while also caring about their own sustainability. Collaborative Overload is what happens when organizations reward only the first half of that equation.)
