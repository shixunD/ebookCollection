The Longing Signal
By [inspired by Susan Cain, Bittersweet, 2022]

You are driving home on a Tuesday night when a song comes on the radio — one you haven't heard in years. It lasts three minutes and forty seconds. By the time it ends, your chest feels cracked open. You're not sad, exactly. You're not happy. You're something the English language doesn't have a clean word for.

Susan Cain does. She calls it bittersweet.

Most of us have been trained, from childhood, to treat this feeling as a problem to solve. We interpret the ache as a signal that something is missing, something is wrong, something must be fixed. We reach for our phones. We refresh our feeds. We schedule one more thing. Anything to outrun the hollow yearning at the center of our chest.

But what if the ache isn't the problem? What if it's the signal?

## What the Research Actually Shows

In a study at the University of Jyvaskyla in Finland, researchers played 102 participants music from the Band of Brothers soundtrack — the kind of minor-key, quietly devastating score that makes you feel grief for things you haven't lost. The strongest predictor of whether someone was moved to tears? Not how much loss they'd experienced. Not their current mood. It was empathy — specifically, their capacity to feel connected to other human beings.

The people who wept weren't the most broken in the room. They were, by measure, the most emotionally alive.

Susan Cain spent years researching why so many of us feel inexplicably elevated by sad music, rainy afternoons, and old photographs of places we've never been. What she found reversed a lot of what mainstream positive psychology has spent decades insisting: the melancholic impulse — what she calls bittersweetness — is not a deficit of joy. It's a different, deeper form of it.

Here's the counterintuitive part. In one study, participants who listened to bittersweet songs reported more listening sessions than those who preferred happy music — not just more, but nearly five times more. 800 average plays versus 175. People don't endure sad music. They return to it, over and over, because it gives them something that upbeat songs cannot: a sense of profound connection, of common humanity, of what Cain calls the recognition that the world is sacred and mysterious, enchanted even.

In other words, your inexplicable attachment to the Moonlight Sonata isn't nostalgia sickness. It's your nervous system reaching for transcendence.

## The Tyranny of Normative Sunshine

Harvard psychologist Susan David surveyed more than 70,000 people and found that one-third of us judge ourselves for having negative emotions — sadness, grief, longing — and that we extend this judgment to our children. We've built workplaces, schools, and social media cultures organized entirely around the performance of enthusiasm. Stanford students call it Duck Syndrome: gliding smoothly above the surface while paddling like hell below it.

Cain argues that this tyranny of positivity doesn't make us happier. It makes us lonelier. Because what we're actually suppressing when we suppress sadness isn't weakness — it's our capacity for connection. The two-year-old on YouTube crying at Beethoven's Moonlight Sonata didn't need to be corrected. He was, in that moment, the most fully human person in the room.

Dacher Keltner, the Berkeley psychologist who helped Pixar build the emotional architecture of Inside Out, told Cain something that stopped her mid-sentence: "Sadness is like a meditation on compassion. You have this burst of — there's harm there, there's need there. I've always felt that way about the human condition. I'm not a tragic person. I'm hopeful. But I think sadness is beautiful and sadness is wise."

This, from one of the world's leading researchers on joy and awe.

## Longing Is Not Passive

The word longing derives from the Old English langian — to grow long, to reach, to extend. The German langen means to reach toward. In Hebrew, the root word for longing shares its origins with the word for passion.

Notice what all of these mean: movement. Reaching. Extension. Not retreat.

We've been sold a cultural story that goes: positive emotions are productive, negative emotions are obstructions. Get rid of sadness, maximize happiness, optimize your state. But Cain's research — drawing on Aristotle, C.S. Lewis, the Sufi tradition, and contemporary neuroscience — suggests a different architecture. Longing is momentum in disguise. The ache of not-yet-there is the very engine of creativity, connection, and meaning.

Nick Cave, the musician, put it plainly: "My artistic life has centered around the desire — or, more accurately, need — to articulate the feelings of loss and longing that have whistled through my bones and hummed in my blood." He didn't resolve the longing. He built a career from it.

Abraham Lincoln. Maya Angelou. Nina Simone. Aristotle noticed 2,000 years ago that the great poets, philosophers, and leaders often had melancholic personalities. He didn't know why. We are only beginning to understand: the capacity to sit with yearning without immediately resolving it is the same capacity that produces empathy, creativity, and the willingness to care about things beyond yourself.

## What This Means for You

If you are the kind of person who gets quietly wrecked by certain songs, who finds rainy afternoons inexplicably peaceful, who tears up at graduation ceremonies for children that aren't yours — you are not broken. You are not depressed. You are not someone whose emotional regulation needs improving.

You are someone whose signal is working.

The question isn't how to turn the ache off. The question is what it's pointing toward. Because underneath every longing — for a place, a person, a life you haven't built yet — is information about what you most deeply value. It's the soul's version of compass calibration.

The bittersweet moment isn't a detour from meaning. It is the signal that meaning is nearby.

---
Today's action: The next time you feel that inexplicable ache — a song, a photograph, a smell that takes you somewhere — don't immediately distract yourself. Sit with it for 60 seconds and ask: what is this pointing toward? What do I care about so much that its absence makes me ache?

📌 Takeaway: The melancholy you were trained to suppress is not a malfunction. It is your most honest signal about what you love and what you are reaching toward — which is also, quietly, who you are.

💡 Extension: Arthur Brooks in The Happiness Files notes that suppressing negative emotions at work creates what he calls "chronic suffering" — the kind that never gets named, never gets processed, and therefore never heals. Cain and Brooks arrive at the same conclusion from different directions: you can't skip the bitter and keep the sweet.
