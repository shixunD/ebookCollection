The Skill Nobody Taught You (But Everyone Needs)

In the mid-1960s, a researcher named Mihaly Csikszentmihalyi walked into an art school in Chicago with a simple experiment and left with a result that still unsettles people decades later.

He set up a table covered with twenty-seven objects — bones, velvet, books, a glass prism, a worn hat. He invited art students to select any items they wanted, arrange them into a still life, and draw. What he was actually watching was not the drawing. He was watching how students approached the table.

Some moved quickly. They picked up a few objects, settled on an arrangement almost immediately, and got to work. Others were slower. They picked up object after object, turned each one over, tried configurations and abandoned them, rearranged again and again. The first group, as Csikszentmihalyi saw it, was solving a problem: how do I produce a good drawing? The second group was still finding the problem — they had not yet decided what the drawing should actually be about.

When a panel of art experts later rated the finished pieces without knowing their source, the problem finders' work was judged significantly more creative. And when Csikszentmihalyi followed up with the same students eighteen years later, the results were even sharper: the problem finders had become the successful professional artists. The problem solvers had largely left the field.

Daniel Pink, drawing on this research in To Sell Is Human, argues that this distinction — between solving problems and finding them — is now the central skill divide in modern work. For most of the twentieth century, access to information was the bottleneck. Whoever controlled information controlled influence. But in a world where information is democratized, where any buyer can research any product before a conversation begins, the old information advantage has evaporated. What remains scarce is the capacity to identify the problem the other person does not know they have.

This is what Pink calls clarity — and it operates differently from what most of us were trained to do.

Traditional education, from grade school through graduate school, is built almost entirely on problem solving. A problem is defined, constraints are given, and students are evaluated on the quality of their solution. The better you are at working within defined parameters, the higher you score. This system produces competent executors. It is not designed to produce people who question whether the defined parameters are the right ones.

The Conference Board surveyed 155 public school superintendents and asked them to rank the cognitive skills most important in today's workforce. They ranked problem solving first. Then the same survey went to eighty-nine employers. Their top-ranked skill? Problem identification.

Those two groups are not answering the same question. And that gap is where a great deal of professional value either gets created or gets lost.

Consider what this looks like in practice. A teacher who asks why students are disengaged — and questions whether the curriculum itself is the wrong frame — is finding a problem. A teacher who tries harder strategies within a curriculum that was never right for these students is solving a problem that was never the real one. Both are working hard. Only one is working on the thing that actually needs changing.

Pink illustrates this with an example from Palantir Technologies, a company that employs no traditional salespeople. Instead it deploys what it calls forward-deployed engineers — technical experts who go into the field, live inside client problems, and surface challenges the clients themselves had not articulated. The most important thing they do, Palantir's Shyam Sankar told Pink, is find the right problems to solve. Not pitch solutions. Find problems.

This reframe matters at a deeper level than professional skill. It is about epistemic humility — recognizing that the presenting complaint is rarely the actual issue. Psychologists see this every day. A client comes in describing anxiety about a job interview. The actual issue, which only emerges over time, is a fear of being seen as fraudulent that has been organizing their behavior for years. The problem they came in with was real, but it was a symptom. The problem worth finding was behind it.

The same logic applies in classrooms, in organizations, in relationships. What looks like a motivation problem is often a meaning problem. What looks like a communication breakdown is often a misaligned assumption about what the relationship is for.

Getting better at problem finding requires a specific kind of patience — what Csikszentmihalyi's students demonstrated at the table of objects. It means resisting the pull toward premature resolution. It means staying in the discomfort of not yet knowing what you are really trying to solve, long enough for the real problem to emerge.

This is not a soft skill. It is one of the hardest cognitive practices there is. Our brains are trained to close open loops, to relieve the tension of ambiguity by moving toward answers. Problem finding asks you to keep the loop open a little longer than is comfortable.

The students who lingered at the table were not less capable than those who moved quickly. They were exercising a discipline the fast movers had skipped.

Today's action: Take one problem you are currently working on — at work, in your teaching, in a relationship. Write down the problem as you have been framing it. Then ask: what if this is not the real problem? What would the problem look like one level deeper? Give yourself five minutes with that question before returning to your current approach.

📌 Takeaway: The most valuable professional skill in the modern world is not solving the problem in front of you — it is questioning whether that is the right problem at all. Problem finding is rare, trainable, and almost entirely absent from formal education.

💡 Extension: Adam Grant in Think Again echoes this from a different angle — arguing that the willingness to question your own working assumptions is a foundational intellectual habit, and that most of us are trained to be good lawyers defending our existing views rather than scientists testing them.
