You're Confusing Contempt With Strength

I want to tell you something uncomfortable: the person you're most angry at right now isn't the problem. The problem is what your anger has turned into.

There's a difference between disagreement and contempt that most of us miss because we lump them together. Anger is clean. Disagreement is functional. You can get angry at someone, hear them out, and change your mind. Anger is a response to a threat or offense. It doesn't require you to believe the other person is fundamentally less than you.

Contempt does. And contempt is a poison.

Arthur C. Brooks, the behavioral scientist who has spent decades studying what actually predicts human flourishing, opens Love Your Enemies with a finding that should stop you cold: contempt is the only negative emotion reliably linked to both physical illness and relationship dissolution. Not anger. Not conflict. Contempt. In a landmark study by John Gottman on what predicts divorce, contempt showed up in the data like a death knell—if it entered a marriage, the relationship was essentially over. Couples could argue, disagree, fight, and still recover. But once contempt set in—once one partner's dominant emotion shifted from "I'm angry at you" to "I'm disgusted by you," to "you're not worth taking seriously"—the relationship had already ended. The paperwork was just a formality.

The physiological consequences are just as stark. Studies on long-term contempt show cortisol elevation, immune suppression, and inflammatory markers that look remarkably similar to chronic stress disease. Holding onto contempt for a person—a group, a political opponent, a family member—is essentially a slow-motion form of self-poisoning. You think you're punishing them. You're actually poisoning yourself.

Here's the part most of us get wrong: we don't recognize contempt when we're feeling it because we call it something else. We call it conviction. We call it principle. We call it having standards. We describe it as "I just can't respect someone who thinks/does/believes that." And boom—we've turned our judgment about an idea into a judgment about their humanity. The person who disagrees with you politically? They're not just wrong. They're contemptible. Not worth listening to. Fundamentally lesser. Deserving of mockery rather than engagement.

This is where the language matters. When you say someone is "stupid" or "evil" or "beneath consideration," you're not stating a fact. You're describing a relationship shift. You've moved from opposing their argument to opposing their existence as a person worth taking seriously. And once you're there, something inside you closes off. The part that learns. The part that stays curious. The part that remains flexible.

Brooks argues that this contempt-collapse is accelerated by something structural in modern life: we are increasingly surrounded by people who confirm our contempt. Social media algorithms serve you the most enraging version of people you disagree with. Your news feed shows you the stupidest person on the other side, not the most thoughtful. You get better at despising an imaginary opponent than understanding an actual one. Contempt becomes both the emotion and the constant input. It becomes your default.

The tragic part is that contempt feels like strength. It feels like you have high standards. It feels like you're protecting something important. And in that feeling, most people stop. They don't examine whether the contempt is actually making them smarter, kinder, or more right. They just know it feels righteous.

But righteous is not the same as right.

The question Brooks pushes at is this: What is contempt actually doing for you? Because from a pure survival standpoint, it does one thing very well—it makes you feel morally superior in the moment. It gives you the hit of being above someone else. But that's a short-term win in a long-term game. It closes your mind. It makes you incapable of learning from the person you despise. It makes you sick. And it teaches everyone around you that this is how you treat people when you decide they're not worth your respect.

There's a way to disagree deeply—even bitterly—without contempt. It requires something most of us have gotten out of the practice of doing: it requires assuming that the person across from you is reasoning from different information or different values, not that they're defective.

A person can be completely wrong and still be worthy of your attention. A person can believe something you find repugnant and still contain aspects of wisdom you haven't discovered yet. A person can be your enemy and still be, in some strange way, essential to you—not because you should befriend them, but because they force you to examine what you actually believe and why.

This doesn't mean you have to agree. It doesn't mean you soften your stance. Brooks is not suggesting that contempt is bad because niceness is always better. He's suggesting it's bad because it literally damages your brain and your capacity to think clearly. And that's a different argument entirely. This is self-interest dressed up as principle.

The shift from contempt back to disagreement is small but requires intention. It's the difference between "that person is stupid" and "that person reached a different conclusion." It's the difference between "they're evil" and "I think they're wrong and here's why." It's the difference between mockery and argument.

One keeps you locked in contempt. The other keeps you capable of learning.

The person you're most contemptuous of right now isn't beneath you. They're just disagreeing with you from a different place. And the moment you remember that—the moment you can hold a strong opinion without descending into contempt for the person holding the other one—you've kept a part of yourself alive that contempt kills.

That's not weakness. That's survival.

---

Today's action: Think of one person or group you currently feel contempt for—someone you dismiss, mock, or consider beneath serious consideration. Write down what you actually think they're wrong about (not why they're a bad person). See if you can state their best argument in a way they'd recognize it. Notice what happens in your body when you do this exercise. Contempt lives in dismissal. Disagreement lives in engagement.

Takeaway: Contempt feels like strength because it gives you the high of moral superiority, but it's slowly poisoning your health and your thinking. Disagreement, maintained without contempt, keeps you capable of learning from your enemies.

Extension: Paul Bloom in Against Empathy makes a related argument—that feeling contempt for certain people (those we judge as undeserving of empathy) actually narrows our moral vision. The constraint isn't empathy; it's contempt masquerading as discernment. Also, Walter Sinnott-Armstrong in Think Again argues that the people most capable of changing their minds are those who can engage opposition without requiring that opposition to be contemptible—they separate the idea from the person.
