A teacher hands back a test. One student sees a B and thinks, "I'm not a math person." Another sees the same B and thinks, "I didn't study the right way." Ten years later, the first student stops trying. The second becomes an engineer.

The difference isn't intelligence. It's how they interpret what intelligence is.

Adam Grant's *Hidden Potential* opens with a deceptively simple question: What if we've been measuring the wrong thing? Not because we lack good metrics, but because we've fundamentally misunderstood what determines whether someone succeeds. We've built an entire educational and professional system around the assumption that potential is fixed—that you're either a "math person" or you're not, that you're naturally creative or naturally analytical, that some people are just born leaders and others aren't. And then we're baffled when people don't reach what we thought was their ceiling.

The research is stark. In one classic study, teachers were told (falsely) that certain students in their classes were "intellectual bloomers"—kids who were expected to show dramatic gains. By the end of the year, those students had indeed made larger gains. Not because anything about them changed. Because their teachers' expectations changed, and lower-friction interactions followed.

This is the inverse of the fixed mindset that Carol Dweck famously studied—the "I'm not a math person" mentality. But there's a second, more subtle form of fixed thinking that Grant explores: we're also prone to underestimate how much people can *stretch*, grow, and reinvent themselves *given the right conditions*. And we fail to ask the crucial question: What are those conditions?

Here's where it gets dangerous. We assume that if someone hasn't shown hidden potential yet, it's probably not there. A colleague isn't assertive enough to lead. A friend isn't disciplined enough to lose weight. A teenage kid isn't curious enough to ever get good at science. But what we're really observing is someone in their current context, with their current supports, their current sense of what's possible. Change the context, and you might change everything.

Grant's research suggests that hidden potential emerges through three conditions. First: learning from feedback, even critical feedback. Not the kind of feedback that crushes you (that's demoralizing). But the kind that comes from someone who believes in you *and* is honest about where you need to improve. Second: role models and reference points that expand your sense of what's possible. You can't become what you can't imagine. And third: opportunity to practice in low-stakes settings before high-stakes ones. This is why the musicians who become virtuosos aren't necessarily the most talented kids at age 10—they're often the kids whose parents forced them to keep playing, even when they weren't naturally inclined, until suddenly they were.

But here's the painful part: we're terrible at recognizing these conditions in real time. A struggling performer gets critical feedback without belief in their potential—that's demoralizing, and they retreat. They don't get exposure to role models because we assume there's no point. They don't get low-stakes practice opportunities because why waste time on someone who "probably doesn't have it"?

And then we're surprised when they never bloom.

The research on what Grant calls "scaffolding" is illuminating. When you combine support with stretch—when you give someone a genuinely hard challenge but also concrete help reaching for it—something shifts. But we tend to do the opposite. We give low-potential candidates the easy work (because they're "not ready" for the hard stuff) and the high-potential ones the hard work (because "they can handle it"). The result: high-potential people get better at doing hard things. Low-potential people get better at nothing, because they never have to try.

This logic extends beyond individuals. It extends to how we think about potential in groups, in organizations, even in ourselves. You tell yourself, "I'm not a public speaker" because you haven't practiced public speaking with good feedback and people who believed you could do it. So you avoid speaking. And then you become worse at speaking, which confirms your belief. The fixed mindset becomes self-fulfilling.

What makes this worse is that we apply this logic asymmetrically. We're often charitable about our own constraints. ("I didn't have time to learn guitar, but I *could* if I wanted to.") But ruthless about others' limits. ("He's just not a people person.") This is partly ego protection. But it's also a profound failure of imagination about what humans can do.

Grant cites research on what's called "grit," but argues (rightly) that grit isn't a fixed trait either. You don't have grit or you don't. You have grit in contexts where you believe the effort will pay off, where you have people cheering you on, where you can see progress. In contexts where the deck feels stacked, where nobody believes in you, where progress is invisible? You give up. And we call that low grit. When really, we've designed a situation where grit becomes irrational.

This is especially important for those of us who mentor, teach, lead, or parent. The default move when someone is struggling is to lower the bar, give them easier work, remove them from challenging contexts. It's kindness masquerading as realism. But it's actually the *opposite* of generosity. Real generosity is believing someone can reach farther than they currently can, and then giving them the specific support to do it.

This doesn't mean every person can become every thing. Some limits are real. But the limits we perceive are almost always larger than the limits that actually exist. And we treat our perceptions as if they're facts.

📌 **Takeaway:** Think of someone in your life—a colleague, a friend, a family member—whom you've written off as "not good at X." (Speaking up, taking initiative, learning new skills, whatever.) What if your assessment is less about their actual potential and more about the context they're in when you observe them? What would it look like to give them critical feedback *and* clear belief in their ability to improve? To expose them to role models who are doing what you think they can't? To let them practice in low-stakes settings first? Don't do this performatively or patronizingly. But actually try one condition, and see what shifts.

💡 **Extension:** Arthur C. Brooks, in *From Strength to Strength*, explores the inverse insight—that we often *overestimate* where our talents lie early in our careers, leading us to optimize for the wrong strengths. Morgan Housel's *The 5 Types of Wealth* touches on a related theme: potential isn't fixed, but we optimize for the wrong forms of capital (money vs. time, social bonds, knowledge) early on. Carol Dweck's original growth mindset research laid groundwork here, but Grant's contribution is showing that mindset alone isn't enough—you need context, support, and strategic practice.
