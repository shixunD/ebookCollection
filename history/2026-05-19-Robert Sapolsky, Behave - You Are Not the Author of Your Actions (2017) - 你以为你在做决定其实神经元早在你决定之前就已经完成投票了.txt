You Are Not the Author of Your Actions
Inspired by Robert Sapolsky, Behave (2017)

One second before you do anything — before you speak, decide, lose your temper, offer help, fall in love — your brain has already made its move.

That is not a metaphor. It is biology.

Robert Sapolsky spent forty years as a neuroendocrinologist studying why humans and other primates behave the way they do. His answer, accumulated across thousands of pages of Behave, is quietly devastating: the behavior you think you chose was already set in motion by a cascade of causes you never controlled — firing neurons, hormonal shifts, sensory input from the past few seconds, memories encoded years ago, the neighborhood you grew up in, the culture that shaped your grandparents. Every layer of causation extends further back than your conscious awareness can reach.

In a now-famous series of experiments, neuroscientist Benjamin Libet measured brain activity while participants decided to flick their wrist. He found that the brain's motor cortex activated on average 300 to 500 milliseconds before participants were conscious of deciding to move. The decision appeared to happen in the brain before it arrived in awareness. Libet's results sparked one of the most contentious debates in modern neuroscience — but Sapolsky's synthesis goes further. He argues it is not just one moment of decision that precedes awareness. It is everything. The person who acts aggressively after a night of bad sleep is not choosing aggression — they are producing it from a cortisol-flooded, prefrontal-cortex-compromised brain. The person who shows extraordinary generosity under stress may have oxytocin receptors shaped by early childhood attachment. These are not poetic descriptions. They are measurable, replicable findings.

The implication that most people instinctively resist is this: if you follow the causal chain far enough back, there is no uncaused moment. No point at which a free, autonomous self stepped outside biology and history and made a clean choice.

Now pause before you dismiss this. Because Sapolsky is not saying nothing matters. He is saying something far more useful: judgment — of others and of yourself — needs to become more accurate.

Here is where it gets practical. Consider how you behave when someone cuts you off in traffic, fails to meet a deadline, or says something hurtful at dinner. The automatic human response is to attribute their behavior to character. They are reckless. Irresponsible. Unkind. Social psychologists call this the fundamental attribution error (FAE) — our tendency to explain other people's actions through stable personality traits while explaining our own through situational context. I was rude because I was stressed. They were rude because that is who they are.

Sapolsky's neuroscience gives the FAE a biological foundation. The other person, like you, was shaped by forces cascading backward through time — genetics, stress hormones, early experience, sleep quality last night, a comment someone made to them three hours ago. Their "character" is not an independent essence. It is a pattern produced by all of that. Which means harsh judgment is, more often than not, a failure of understanding rather than a moral exercise.

This is not an excuse for harmful behavior. Sapolsky makes the distinction carefully. Society still needs consequences — not because people deserve punishment in some cosmic sense, but because consequences themselves become part of the causal chain that shapes future behavior. Accountability is real and important. What becomes harder to sustain, under his framework, is contempt. The story that some people are simply bad, fundamentally broken, beyond consideration.

Adam Grant makes a related move in Think Again — he argues that seeing people as capable of change is not naive optimism, it is the empirically more accurate position. Combined with Sapolsky's framework, the picture becomes clear: the person in front of you is a product of causes, many of which were never in their control. Responding to them with curiosity rather than condemnation is not weakness. It is precision.

And it applies inward, too. The self-criticism you carry for a pattern you cannot seem to break — the procrastination, the reactivity, the chronic self-doubt — may deserve less moral weight than you have been assigning it. Understanding the causes of your behavior is not a reason to stop working on it. It is a reason to approach that work with less shame and more strategy.

You are not the helpless puppet of your neurons. But you are also not the sole, uncaused author of your actions. You are something in between — a person shaped by enormous forces, with a genuine but limited capacity to redirect them. That is humbling. It is also, strangely, a relief.

---

Today's action: Think of one person whose behavior has frustrated you recently. Spend two minutes asking: what forces — stress, history, biology, circumstance — might have produced that behavior? Notice whether your response to them shifts even slightly.

📌 Takeaway: Harsh judgment — of others or yourself — is often not moral clarity. It is just a failure to follow the causal chain far enough back.

💡 Extension: (Adam Grant in Think Again offers a similar angle — he argues that the most accurate view of people is that they are capable of change, which maps directly onto Sapolsky's point that behavior is produced by modifiable causes, not fixed character.)
