The Serenity of Accepting What Won't Change
Inspired by Morgan Housel, Same as Ever (2023)

Here is a question that sounds almost too simple: What if the most powerful thing you could do today is stop trying to fix something?

Not because you are lazy. Not because you have given up. But because some things — by their very nature — will not change. And the energy you spend fighting them is energy that never comes back.

Morgan Housel opens Same as Ever with a deceptively quiet observation: the world changes constantly, but human behavior barely changes at all. We are wired for overconfidence, for short-term thinking, for comparing ourselves to our neighbors, for assuming that this time is different. These tendencies are not modern problems. They appear in Roman letters, in medieval trade records, in 1920s newspaper editorials. They are, as Housel puts it, permanent features of the human operating system.

That insight is uncomfortable — because we live in a culture obsessed with optimization. We are told that with enough information, the right framework, the correct intervention, we can change almost anything. Fix the behavior. Redesign the incentive. Run the experiment. Adjust. Iterate. Improve.

And much of the time, that is true. Some things genuinely change. But Housel's deeper point is that some things emphatically do not — and that confusing the two categories is the source of enormous, unnecessary suffering.

Psychologist Steven Hayes, the founder of Acceptance and Commitment Therapy (ACT), spent decades studying why smart, motivated people stay stuck. His answer was counterintuitive: they get stuck not from lack of effort, but from too much struggle against things that cannot be controlled. He called this psychological inflexibility — the refusal to accept what is already true. The more fiercely you resist an unchangeable reality, the more cognitive bandwidth it consumes, and the less capacity you have to act on things that actually matter.

Hayes did not mean passive resignation. He meant something more precise: distinguishing between what you can influence and what you cannot, and then — crucially — accepting the second category not reluctantly, but completely. Only when you stop draining energy into the unchangeable can you redirect it toward the genuinely movable.

Housel frames this in financial and historical terms. Markets will panic. Recessions will arrive. Humans will be greedy near peaks and terrified near bottoms. These are not bugs in the system. They are the system. The investor who accepts this — who builds a strategy around permanent human irrationality rather than assuming it away — tends to outperform the one who keeps being surprised by it. Not because they are smarter. Because they are not fighting the weather.

This applies far beyond finance. Think about the person who has spent five years trying to change a difficult family member. Or the leader who is exhausted from fighting a toxic institutional culture they have no power to redesign. Or the teacher who measures her worth by whether every student finds the material compelling. The suffering in each case is real. But a significant portion of it is generated not by the situation itself — but by the gap between what is and what one believes should be.

The Stoics understood this. Marcus Aurelius wrote in Meditations: "You have power over your mind, not outside events. Realize this, and you will find strength." Epictetus, a former slave, built an entire philosophy around the dichotomy of control: some things are in our power, some are not, and wisdom is knowing the difference. What is remarkable is that this was not a counsel of defeat. Both men led active, demanding lives. Accepting what could not change gave them the clarity to act more forcefully on what could.

Housel's version of this insight is less philosophical, more behavioral: we are all swimming in a river with permanent currents. The strongest swimmers are not the ones who fight the current the hardest. They are the ones who know where it runs, accept it as a fact of the river, and choose their line accordingly.

The trap is that accepting unchangeable things feels like failure in a society that celebrates relentless effort. We have been trained to believe that persistence always pays, that giving up is weakness, that anything can be fixed with enough willpower. But that training was never precise enough. It forgot to teach us the other half: some things are fixed. Recognizing this is not surrender. It is the beginning of usable clarity.

You probably have one or two items on your internal worry list that belong in this category. Things you have argued about, fretted over, or tried to solve for years — with diminishing returns and real emotional cost. They are not going to change. And the first step toward peace is admitting that clearly, not bitterly.

---

Today's action: Write down one thing you have been trying to change for more than a year with little progress. Ask honestly: Is this in my control? If not, write one sentence accepting it as a permanent feature — and note one thing the freed energy could go toward instead.

📌 Takeaway: The most underrated productivity move is not another system or habit — it is clearly identifying what will not change and fully releasing your grip on it.

💡 Extension: (Arthur Brooks in From Strength to Strength offers a similar view — he argues that the second half of life becomes lighter not when circumstances improve, but when you stop treating impermanence and decline as problems to be solved rather than realities to be integrated.)
