There's a moment that defines adults: the moment you realize being right has become more important to you than being true. Walter Sinnott-Armstrong, a philosopher who studies how we change our minds, calls this "intellectual entrenchment." I call it the most expensive habit you'll ever develop.

Most people assume changing your mind is something that just happens to you—new evidence arrives, you shift, life goes on. But Sinnott-Armstrong's research in Think Again suggests something darker: changing your mind is actually an achievement, not a default. And you have to be willing to pay for it.

Here's the cost. When you genuinely change your mind, you're not just updating your beliefs. You're admitting that your past self was wrong. And not in the small way—"oh, I misread that detail"—but in the way that touches identity. If you've spent five years saying X and now you believe Y, you have to live with the reality that you spent five years being wrong. Most people can't afford that psychologically. So they don't change their minds. They rationalize. They find evidence that supports what they already think. They call this "being consistent," but it's actually just self-protection wearing a thinking cap.

The research is brutal here. Sinnott-Armstrong studied how people respond when presented with evidence that contradicts their core beliefs. The brain has a defense mechanism—it literally works harder when threatened with disconfirming evidence, not to update the belief but to reinforce it. You don't consciously decide this. Your neurobiology does it for you. Your brain is protecting you from the psychic wound of being wrong.

But here's what he argues—and what breaks open the standard narrative: this protection is expensive. It costs you. Not financially, but cognitively. Every belief you've defended past its expiration date is a commitment you're making to a version of yourself that's increasingly outdated.

Think about this practically. Someone in your life—maybe you—held a firm opinion about a parent, a partner, a career path, a political position, ten years ago. They gathered evidence to support it. They built arguments. They positioned themselves against the alternative. And maybe new information arrived that would have suggested a different conclusion. But changing would mean admitting they'd been wrong for ten years. Most people can't do it. So they dig deeper. They find better arguments. They find other people who agree. And they become more entrenched, not because they're stupid, but because they're trying to protect something that feels like their self.

The irony Sinnott-Armstrong keeps returning to: the people who are most resistant to changing their minds are usually the most intelligent. They're just smart enough to build airtight defenses. They know how to argue. They understand rhetoric. They can out-think you in a debate. What they can't do—what intelligence doesn't help with—is admit they were wrong.

But here's where it gets interesting. He suggests that changing your mind is actually a learned skill, not an innate capacity. You can get better at it. And the people who get good at it share one thing: they've developed an ability to separate their belief from their identity. They can hold a thought and not treat that thought as them.

Most of us do the opposite. We say "I believe X" and we really mean "I am the kind of person who believes X." X becomes a marker of who we are. To abandon X is to abandon a part of our self-concept. And that's too threatening. So we don't. We die with the same set of beliefs we adopted in our twenties because at some point those beliefs became synonymous with ourselves.

Sinnott-Armstrong offers a different move: What if beliefs are tools instead of identity markers? A tool you use as long as it works, and when you have evidence it doesn't work, you swap it out. No trauma. No identity crisis. Just a practical upgrade. Your screwdriver worked great for five years, but this new situation calls for a wrench. You don't defend the screwdriver. You just use the wrench.

The catch—and this is what most people miss—is that living like this requires a specific kind of confidence. It's not confidence that you're right. It's confidence that you can change. It's confidence that your worth isn't determined by any single belief you happen to hold today. That's a different spine. That's the kind of confidence that actually holds up in reality because it's not defending anything. It's just available for what comes next.

I think about my own resistance to changing my mind. It's usually strongest around the things I've publicly committed to, the things I've built identity around. The positions I've argued for. The people I've dismissed. These are hard to reverse not because the evidence is unclear, but because reversing them costs something real: it costs the story I've been telling about who I am.

But Sinnott-Armstrong keeps returning to this: the alternative to changing your mind is calcifying your mind. And a calcified mind is a finished mind. It's no longer learning because learning requires the willingness to be wrong, to discover that what you thought doesn't hold up, to let that discovery change the shape of who you are.

The people who have the best lives—not the most comfortable lives, but the ones with the most depth and adaptability—are the ones who've learned to change their minds relatively early in the process, before the belief becomes load-bearing in their identity. They don't wait until it's a crisis. They've normalized the feeling of being wrong.

This is the luxury of the intellectually flexible: you can course-correct without it feeling like a betrayal of yourself.

📌 Takeaway: What's one belief you held confidently five years ago that doesn't fit reality anymore? Not a casual opinion, but something you actually organized your decisions around. Write out the evidence that originally convinced you it was true, then write out why you now know it doesn't hold. What did that change cost you? What did it open up? This week, practice identifying one belief you're currently defending hard—not just discussing, but defending—and ask honestly: Am I defending this because it's true, or because I'm identified with it?

💡 Extension: Adam Grant in Originals argues that non-conformists often fail because they lack the humility to revisit their own ideas—Sinnott-Armstrong suggests the inverse: changing your mind is the highest form of intelligence, not a failure of conviction.
