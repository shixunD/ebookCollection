The most unsettling finding from the regret research isn't what people regret—it's *when* they regret it. Amy Summerville and colleagues at Miami University have spent years asking people to describe their biggest life regrets, and the pattern is stark: what haunts you depends almost entirely on your age. We don't have a single regret curve; we have a regret age curve, and it reveals something we spend years trying not to see.

In your twenties and thirties, regrets are almost entirely about inaction. The thing you didn't try. The conversation you didn't start. The place you didn't go because it seemed impractical. The relationship you didn't pursue because the timing felt off. The career pivot you considered but decided was too risky. Young people regret the roads not taken far more than the roads taken and found wanting.

But something shifts around 40. The regret profile changes. It's not that inaction stops mattering, but a new category of regret emerges with real force: the sunk-cost regret. You regret the time and energy you poured into the thing that didn't pan out. You regret that you didn't quit sooner. You regret that you stayed in that relationship, that job, that city, that version of yourself, convinced you could fix it, when the time for fixing it had already passed.

Then something darker still emerges in the 50s and 60s. Summerville calls this "the finality regret." It's not "I wish I had done this," but "I wish I still could." The regret is no longer theoretical. It's not about a choice you could still reverse. It's about recognizing that the window has closed. You can't un-live your forties. You can't get back the creativity you deferred or the relationships you neglected. The saddest regrets are the ones where the person understands, finally, that they're too old to recover from the mistake.

What makes this research particularly cruel is how preventable so many of these regrets are. The inaction regrets in your twenties? Those are within your control. You have time to change your mind, to pursue the thing, to move, to try. But you don't pursue it because the future feels infinite, and this particular thing—while interesting—competes with immediate comfort, immediate safety, immediate approval from people whose opinion probably shouldn't be that heavy on your decision-making.

The forty-something sunk-cost regrets are preventable too, but in a different way. They're preventable if you have the emotional clarity to cut your losses when a situation is no longer serving you. But that clarity is often obscured by the logic that "I've already invested this much," which Summerville's work suggests is one of the most expensive mistakes people make. The research on decision-making shows that people are horrifyingly bad at abandoning failed courses of action because admitting failure feels like admitting weakness. So you persist through things you should quit, and then spend 20 years regretting that you didn't quit sooner.

And the 60-something regrets? Those are the hardest to grapple with because they're genuinely irreversible. The person who spent their forties climbing a ladder that turned out to be leaning against the wrong building now understands that their fifties—the decade where they still had time to pivot, to rebuild, to start fresh—is gone. They made different choices then, and those choices, compounded, have left them somewhere they don't particularly want to be, with limited runway to course-correct.

But there's something else in the research that deserves attention. Summerville and colleagues found that the people with the fewest regrets aren't the ones who made only good decisions. They're the ones who made decisions—good or bad—and then fully committed to them. They didn't hedge. They didn't spend twenty years wondering if they should have chosen differently. They chose, adapted to the choice, and made peace with it. There's almost a grief process to it: the realization that this path means not taking that other path, and that's simply how choices work.

The data suggests something sobering: if you're in your twenties and you have a nagging sense that you should try something risky or unconventional, that voice is probably worth listening to. Not every risky thing will work. But research on regret suggests that the risk of inaction carries a higher emotional tax than the risk of action, even when the action fails. You'll regret what you didn't try with more sting than what you tried and found lacking.

Conversely, if you're 45 and you're in something that isn't working—a career, a relationship, a situation—the research suggests you should pay attention to that signal. The regret cost of staying is likely higher than you're calculating it. You have time still to pivot, to rebuild, to become something different. That window is open, but it has a closing date, and you can feel it ticking if you pay attention.

The hardest regrets to read in Summerville's data are from people who had the chance to choose differently at 45 and chose the comfortable thing instead, then hit 65 with the realization that the comfortable thing was also the limiting thing. That they sacrificed growth for stability, possibility for security, and by the time they realized it was a bad trade, there wasn't enough road left to walk back.

The regret age curve is, in the end, a map of the questions you should be asking yourself at each life stage. In your twenties: What am I not trying because I'm afraid? In your forties: What am I persisting with because I'm invested, not because it's working? In your sixties: Did I make the trades I actually wanted to make?

📌 Takeaway: Today, think about your current age and the typical regret profile for that decade. If you're under 40, identify one inaction that nags at you—not a fantasy, but something concrete within reach. Write down what's stopping you. Is it actually risk, or is it the comfort of not deciding? If you're over 40, ask yourself honestly: am I still in this situation because it's good, or because I'm invested in it? What signal am I not listening to?
