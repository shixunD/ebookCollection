Most advice you've ever received about success can be summed up in one phrase: *never give up*. Your parents said it. Your teachers said it. Every inspirational movie ever made is built on this premise. Someone who wanted something badly enough held on through impossible odds and eventually won. We celebrate the person who endured. We mock the person who quit. We've built an entire mythology around perseverance.

And almost all of it is wrong.

I don't mean that giving up is always the answer. I mean that we've created a binary choice when the actual decision is far more complex. Seth Godin calls this complexity "the Dip," and it's one of the most underutilized frameworks in decision-making.

Here's the basic insight: every worthwhile endeavor has a Dip—a period where the effort required to continue exceeds the immediate reward. The dip separates the people who *should* quit from the people who should persevere. Most people quit in the dip not because they're weak, but because they don't understand what a dip is. They think it's a signal that they chose wrong. So they leave and start something new. Which also has a dip. Which they also quit. This is how people spend years building nothing.

But here's the more important flip side: most people *don't* quit things they actually should quit. They stay in a bad marriage for the kids. They stay in a dead-end job for the paycheck. They stay in a field they hate because they've already invested so much. They confuse sunk cost with future trajectory. Sunk cost—the money, time, and effort already spent—should be irrelevant to your next decision. But we're psychologically wired to feel it as enormous. This means people stick with things far longer than makes rational sense.

The result is a society full of people doing the wrong thing with great determination.

Godin's framework: there's a difference between a *dip* and a *cul-de-sac*. A dip is a temporary period of difficulty before breakthrough. A cul-de-sac is a dead end disguised as a road. The dip rewards perseverance. The cul-de-sac just rewards more time spent in the wrong place. The problem is they feel almost identical when you're in the middle of them.

How do you tell the difference? Godin suggests asking: "Is the market actually rewarding this goal?" Not someday. Right now. Is there actual market evidence that persisting in this direction leads somewhere? Not your hopes. Not your wishes. Evidence.

A pianist training for decades is in a dip—the market exists, concert halls need pianists, your skills are genuinely improving. A pianist training for decades in a style of music nobody attends concerts for is in a cul-de-sac. Both pianists feel like they're persevering. Only one of them is actually making progress toward something real.

Here's where this gets personal: what are you in a dip with, and what are you in a cul-de-sac with? The dip requires commitment. The cul-de-sac requires clarity.

Some research on this: psychologists study something called the "sunk cost fallacy"—the degree to which people throw good money after bad trying to recover previous losses. In studies, people will persist with obviously failed endeavors longer than makes sense, purely because they've already invested. The bigger the investment, the longer they persist. This isn't weakness. It's how human psychology is wired. But it's also why so many lives are spent pursuing the wrong goals with perseverance.

The more powerful framework is this: what would you attempt if you didn't have to be *consistent* with your past? If your past choices didn't matter at all—they simply didn't count—what would you choose right now? Most people, when they ask that question, immediately know the answer. They know what they should quit. They know what they should start. They just don't because the gap between past and present feels too large.

But here's the thing: the gap doesn't matter. What matters is trajectory. Your past is fixed. Your future is still open. The only rational decision is the one that maximizes your future capability and satisfaction, regardless of what you've already sunk into it.

Godin makes another point that's worth sitting with: being the best in the world—the truly world-class—in something requires being the only one willing to persevere through *your specific* dip. Not everyone's dip. Yours. The dip that nobody else in your exact situation has the specific capacity to endure. If the dip is the same for everyone, then it's not a real dip. It's just a requirement. The real dip is the thing that eliminates 99% of competitors because they quit before understanding what they're in.

So the decision isn't "should I quit?" It's "is this dip actually filtering for something valuable, or is this a cul-de-sac that only feels like progress because I'm moving?"

Here's what to try: list three things you're currently committed to—a goal, a relationship, a job, a project. For each one, ask: "Do I know someone else doing this who's succeeded?" "Is there actual evidence of a working path forward?" "Am I improving measurably week to week?" If the answer to all three is no, you might be in a cul-de-sac. If yes, you might be in a dip worth passing through.

Then have the harder conversation: which one should I actually quit? Not which one is hardest? Which one doesn't have a market? Which one have I been justifying with sunk cost?

The decision to quit might be the most courageous thing you can make.

📌 Takeaway: You don't lack perseverance. You lack clarity about which endeavors actually lead somewhere. Most people quit good dips and persist through dead-end cul-de-sacs.

💡 Extension: Barry Schwartz's *Paradox of Choice* connects here—the more options we see, the more we believe we should be able to optimize endlessly. But some choices are better abandoned than perfected. Arthur Brooks in *From Strength to Strength* also resonates: the shift from "producer" to "sage" requires knowing which peaks to leave behind, which pursuits to quit entirely.
