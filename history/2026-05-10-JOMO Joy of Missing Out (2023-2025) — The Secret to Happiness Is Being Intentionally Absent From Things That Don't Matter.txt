Title: JOMO as a Path to Contentment — How Strategic Absence Builds Deeper Happiness Than Constant Presence

---

What if the thing making you unhappy is not that you are missing out on something, but that you have lost the capacity to be absent from anything at all?

The average person now spends six hours and forty minutes of their waking day on screens. If you are part of a digital industry—marketing, technology, media—it is probably higher. But here is what is strange: the happiness research does not correlate with that. More connection does not equal more contentment. In fact, it often correlates with the opposite. There is a documented rise in anxiety, in the fear of missing out, in the feeling that every moment not spent consuming or producing is a moment of waste.

But a parallel body of research has quietly emerged in recent years, one that asks a different question: What if the path to happiness is not through more engagement but through more selective disengagement?

Welcome to JOMO—the Joy of Missing Out.

JOMO is not the absence of desire to participate. That would just be apathy. JOMO is something more specific: it is the positive feeling that comes from intentionally choosing not to do something, knowing that you are missing it, and finding that you are okay with that. It is a form of freedom that the modern world rarely teaches us to practice.

The psychology of this is not intuitive. Conventionally, we think of happiness as acquisition—getting the promotion, going to the party, buying the thing, being included. But research from 2023 to 2025 on JOMO reveals something different: happiness is also, and sometimes more powerfully, about subtraction. About the relief of saying no. About the deliberate choice to be unavailable.

A 2025 study published in the Educational and Developmental Psychologist examined the relationship between JOMO, FOMO (fear of missing out), and mental wellbeing. What they found was a kind of paradox: people with high JOMO actually reported lower levels of anxiety and depression, better self-compassion, higher mindfulness scores, and greater life satisfaction. But they also—and this is the part that seems contradictory—reported some of the same psychological traits as people with high FOMO: they felt the pull of social events, the notification ding of invitations, the whisper that they might be missing something important. The difference was in how they responded. Those with high JOMO experienced that pull and then consciously decided that missing the event was acceptable—even preferable.

Think about what this means for the architecture of your week. The traditional model of happiness suggests you should accept as many invitations as possible, maintain as many relationships as you can, show up for every opportunity. But this actually creates a zero-sum game: every time you say yes to something, you are implicitly saying no to something else. And the implicit refusals tend to be the things that matter most—the book you wanted to read, the conversation with someone you love, the time to think, the space to rest.

A person with high JOMO has inverted this. They say no explicitly and unapologetically, not because they are antisocial but because they have calculated that the cost of constant availability is higher than the cost of missing some things. They have done the math: two truly great conversations with people you love, versus six mediocre conversations at an event. A weekend with no obligations, versus three separate social commitments. An evening alone with a book, versus an opening at a gallery.

The research suggests that this is not a character defect. It is actually associated with stronger self-knowledge, better boundaries, and more sustainable relationships. People with high JOMO tend to maintain fewer but deeper friendships. They are less dependent on external validation. They experience less loneliness, not because they are less social, but because the relationships they do maintain are more intentional and therefore more satisfying.

This connects to something Arthur Brooks calls "strategic unavailability"—the idea that the highest-ROI relationships and work are those you protect time for, not those you fit in around the edges. When you are available to everything, you are deeply available to nothing.

Consider the person who maintains thirty active friendships, responds to every email, attends every work happy hour, accepts every invitation. They believe they are more connected, more included, more alive. But the research on JOMO suggests the opposite experience: they are actually more anxious. Each commitment creates an obligation that crowds out the next one. Each "yes" is a theft from something deeper. And the chronic background hum of should-be-theres becomes a form of constant anxiety—present at the event but thinking about the other event, present on the call but checking the chat, present but never truly present.

The person with high JOMO has made a different calculation. They have decided that presence requires absence. That to be fully there with anyone, you have to be selectively unavailable to everyone else. Their calendar has white space. Their phone stays in another room for certain hours. They say no, clearly and unapologetically, and they have trained their nervous system to not interpret that as social failure but as social self-respect.

The happiness equation is not: more experiences equals more happiness. The equation, according to the latest research, is closer to: fewer, more intentional experiences, plus the absence of the anxious feeling that you should be doing something else, equals more happiness. The absence of the feeling that you should be absent adds up to something like presence.

What makes JOMO so difficult to practice is that it requires going against the grain of a culture built on the opposite assumption. Social media is designed to make you afraid of missing out. Event culture assumes that showing up is always the right choice. Professional expectations treat availability as a proxy for dedication. To practice JOMO is to be publicly, consciously unavailable in a way that reads to others—and sometimes to yourself—as remoteness or lack of ambition.

But here is what the research actually shows: it is the opposite. The people most satisfied with their lives and relationships are not the ones trying to do everything. They are the ones who have gotten genuinely, unapologetically good at saying no.

The deeper insight is this: when you stop trying to be everywhere, you become present where you are. When you give yourself permission to miss things, you relieve the nervous system of the burden of managing constant social threat. When you protect your "no," your "yes" becomes meaningful. And a life made of meaningful yeses, even if there are fewer of them, is a richer, happier, more connected life than a life made of obligatory maybes.

---

📌 Takeaway: Happiness does not come from experiencing more. It comes from being intentionally unavailable for things that do not align with what matters to you. JOMO—the joy of missing out—is associated with lower anxiety, deeper relationships, and higher life satisfaction. The skill is not saying no defensively. It is saying no so clearly that you stop feeling like you are missing out at all.

💡 Extension: This connects directly to Cal Newport's concept of "deep work" from earlier—the idea that meaningful output requires protected, uninterrupted time. JOMO is the relational and psychological parallel: meaningful relationships require protected, undistracted time. You cannot be deeply present with anyone if you are trying to be partially present with everyone.

---

Today's action: Look at your calendar for next week. Identify one social, professional, or personal commitment that you said yes to out of obligation rather than genuine desire. This could be a meeting, an event, a coffee, or a group chat you feel obligated to maintain. Consider saying no, either now or next time it comes up. Notice what you feel. Is it relief? Guilt? Both? The relief part is JOMO.
