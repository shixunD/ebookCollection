Ninety percent of teachers worldwide believe they should match their teaching to students' learning styles. Visual learners get diagrams. Auditory learners get lectures. Kinesthetic learners get hands-on activities. It sounds caring, thoughtful, even scientifically enlightened. There is only one problem with this approach: decades of research show it does not work.

In Hidden Potential, organizational psychologist Adam Grant points to a comprehensive review of the learning styles literature that reached a damning conclusion: there is "disturbingly little" evidence that matching instruction to a student's preferred style produces any learning advantage. In controlled experiments across age groups and subjects, students did not perform better when taught in their preferred mode. Not a little better. Not measurably better at all.

And yet 89 percent of teachers worldwide still believe it. The belief persists not because of evidence but because it feels right — and because it flatters us. Telling someone they are a "visual learner" sounds like personalizing their education. What it actually does is give them a label that locks them into a comfort zone and convinces them the uncomfortable modes are simply "not for them."

Grant's deeper point is harder to hear: the way you prefer to learn is almost certainly the way that requires the least effort from you. And the way that requires the least effort from you is almost certainly the way you will grow the least.

Consider what Grant found when he looked at how polyglots — people who speak five, six, seven languages — actually acquired their skills. Sara Maria Hasbun spent six years studying Spanish in school and came away barely functional. Her father, from El Salvador, had gently suggested she give up. She had all the "wrong" conditions: she was past the supposed critical period, she had struggled her entire academic language career, and she believed she was simply not a language person. What changed was not her aptitude. What changed was her willingness to be uncomfortable.

Polyglots do not succeed because they discovered their "correct" learning modality. They succeed because they stopped trying to learn in the way that felt natural and started throwing themselves into situations where they had no choice but to struggle. Benny Lewis, who speaks over six languages, spent eleven years failing Irish and German in school. His breakthrough came only when he moved abroad and forced himself to speak before he was ready — stumbling, failing, being corrected, and doing it again the next day. His cognitive ability had not changed. His relationship to discomfort had.

This pattern shows up far outside language learning. Grant traces it through chess, music, athletics, and writing. Steve Martin was called "one of the most serious booking mistakes in Los Angeles history" in early reviews. He was learning comedy the way that felt natural — listening to other comedians, studying their timing, imitating their rhythms. But the one mode he had been avoiding was writing. He hated it. It did not come easily. When he finally forced himself to write every day, his act transformed. The discomfort was not a signal that writing was wrong for him. It was a signal that writing was exactly where he needed to go.

Psychologists call this "desirable difficulty" — the counterintuitive finding that introducing obstacles into the learning process actually deepens retention and skill transfer. Robert Bjork at UCLA has spent decades documenting that the conditions under which people feel they are learning best are frequently not the conditions under which they actually learn most. Struggling, making errors, having to reconstruct material from memory — these feel like signs of failure. They are actually signs of growth.

What this means in practice is unsettling. If you are a person who processes information visually, the growth edge is probably in forcing yourself to learn through reading dense text or listening to complex explanations. If you think of yourself as someone who learns by doing, the growth edge may be in sitting with abstract theory long enough to get uncomfortable. The preferred mode is not the enemy. It is simply already been optimized. It has given most of what it can give. The frontier is somewhere that feels harder.

Grant draws a distinction here that philosopher Agnes Callard articulates precisely: the difference between ambition and aspiration. Ambition is about outcomes — the result you want to achieve. Aspiration is about the person you are becoming. When you optimize only for your comfort zone, you are being ambitious in the narrow sense — executing on what you already do well. Aspiration requires something different. It requires being willing to be bad at something, to feel the particular friction of not-yet-knowing, long enough for a new capability to take root.

Raj Chetty's landmark research across 11,000 children in Tennessee schools found something that surprised even him. The children of the best kindergarten teachers did not maintain higher test scores as they moved through school. Their peers caught up. What persisted — and what predicted adult income 2.4 times more powerfully than early academic scores — were behavioral traits: proactiveness, discipline, determination, the capacity to face something hard and keep going. These are not traits you are born with. They are, as Grant argues, skills developed through repeated encounters with difficulty.

The chess team from Harlem Public School 43, the Raging Rooks, beat elite private school teams not because their coach Maurice Ashley discovered their optimal learning styles. He did the opposite. He started teaching chess from the endgame — the most confusing, disorienting place to begin — because he wanted his students to feel the sting of not understanding, and then the satisfaction of breaking through. He was, knowingly or not, engineering desirable difficulty. The players who thrived were not the ones with the most natural talent. They were the ones who became comfortable with being uncomfortable.

This is the real argument against learning styles — not simply that the theory lacks scientific support, but that it points us in the wrong direction entirely. It orients us toward optimizing for existing strengths when the actual opportunity for growth lives in the modes that feel worst to us right now.

There is a version of self-knowledge that is genuinely useful: knowing where you tend to give up, knowing which kinds of difficulty you reflexively avoid, knowing the modes of learning that feel foreign enough to make you want to stop. That friction is information. It is a map to where you have not yet been.

Today's action: Pick one thing you are trying to learn right now. Identify the mode you have been avoiding — writing instead of reading, talking out loud instead of highlighting, drawing instead of listening. Spend twenty minutes doing it that way, badly, without fixing it. Notice what the discomfort actually feels like when you stay with it instead of escape it.

📌 Takeaway: Your preferred learning style is comfort, not growth. The modes that feel worst are often the ones with the most left to give.

💡 Extension:
- Robert Bjork (UCLA) has spent decades documenting "desirable difficulty" — the paradox that the conditions where we feel we learn best are often not where we grow most.
- Morgan Housel in Same as Ever makes a similar structural argument: what looks like inefficiency or waste from the outside is often exactly what makes a system (or person) resilient. Optimization for comfort is optimization against surprise.
- Arthur Brooks in From Strength to Strength warns that doubling down on your strongest existing skills in midlife leads to stagnation — a different angle on the same truth: growth lives at the uncomfortable edge, not in the safe center.
- Carol Dweck's growth mindset research (Mindset, 2006) is the adjacent foundation: she found that people who believe abilities are fixed avoid challenge, while those who believe abilities grow seek it. Grant's learning-styles argument is the practical, behaviorally specific version of Dweck's insight.
