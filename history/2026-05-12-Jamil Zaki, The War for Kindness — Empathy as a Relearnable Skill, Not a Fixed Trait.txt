Empathy has a branding problem. We treat it as innate moral virtue—you're either an empathic person or you're not—when in fact it's a skill that completely atrophies without practice. And we're all collectively atrophying right now.

Jamil Zaki, a neuroscientist studying empathy at Stanford, spent years documenting something counterintuitive: people who believe empathy is fixed are worse at it. They assume their emotional reactions to other people's problems are involuntary and untrainable. A story about suffering either moves them or doesn't. They either "feel someone's pain" or they don't. And if they don't, well, they're just not an empathic person.

This belief is catastrophic because it's wrong, and it stops people from getting better.

Zaki's research shows that empathy is more like a muscle than a personality trait. You can strengthen it or let it atrophy. And right now, most of us are in atrophy mode. We're overwhelmed by others' suffering—endless news feeds of global crisis, local tragedy, workplace conflict—and we've developed a coping mechanism: we've switched empathy off. It's not sustainable to feel everything deeply, so we stop feeling most things at all. We become numb as a survival strategy.

The irony is that this numbness doesn't protect us; it isolates us. Research shows that people who practice empathy—who actively work to understand others' internal experiences—report stronger relationships, better mental health, and surprisingly, more resilience. Empathy isn't a luxury that makes you soft; it's a requirement for actually connecting with people in a way that sustains you through hard things.

Zaki details how to rebuild this skill, and it's simpler and harder than you'd think.

The simplest version: ask yourself about other people's inner lives with genuine curiosity rather than assumption. You're in a meeting and a colleague snaps at you. Your automatic story might be "they're hostile" or "they don't respect me." Empathy practice looks like asking: "What's actually going on in their world right now that might explain their tone?" Not as therapy for them. As clarity for you. Because you're often wrong about others' motivations, and the story you're telling about their behavior is usually about your insecurity, not their intent.

But here's where it gets hard: empathy requires you to sometimes find humanity in people you'd rather dismiss. It requires you to imagine why your difficult boss, your frustrating family member, or the person with whom you strongly disagree might be doing what they're doing. It requires you to hold the possibility that they're struggling and human, not just wrong and annoying.

This is why empathy isn't fashionable right now. Empathy requires extending generosity to people who probably don't deserve it. And we're all tired. We all want to be right. We all want the world to be divided into "good people like us" and "bad people unlike us." Empathy makes that impossible.

Zaki's work suggests that the capacity to do this isn't about being nice or naive. It's about being curious in a very specific way: curious about people's constraints, not just their character. When you meet someone whose values seem opposed to yours, empathy doesn't mean you approve of them. It means you ask: What experiences, fears, or information shaped them into someone who believes that? What would I believe if I'd lived their life?

The research shows something remarkable: people who practice this kind of disciplined empathy don't become naive pushovers. They become more effective. They negotiate better. They parent more effectively. They build coalitions instead of enemies. They make better decisions because they have more complete information about human reality.

And on a deeper level, they're less lonely. Because you can't feel truly seen by others while you're refusing to see them.

📌 **Takeaway:** Tomorrow, pick one person you've dismissed or resented. Spend five minutes writing out: What constraints, fears, or past experiences might explain why they are the way they are? Don't do this to excuse them. Do it to expand your own humanity.

💡 **Extension:** Paul Bloom, in "Against Empathy," makes the counterargument—that empathy can be biased and that we need principled reasoning alongside it. Both Zaki and Bloom are right; the real insight is that empathy is a *skill*, not a character trait, and skills require both intuition and discipline. David Epstein's "Range" also touches on how understanding different perspectives improves decision-making.
