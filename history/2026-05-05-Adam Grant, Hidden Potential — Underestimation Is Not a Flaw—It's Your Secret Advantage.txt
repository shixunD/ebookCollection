Underestimation Is Not a Flaw—It's Your Secret Advantage
Adam Grant, Hidden Potential (2024)
Published: 2026-05-05 (Taipei Time)

---

There is a phenomenon that keeps successful people up at night, though they rarely admit it. They have been overestimated. Someone saw promise in them that exceeded what they actually possessed. And now they live with the quiet terror of being found out.

Adam Grant, in Hidden Potential, flips this frame completely. He argues that one of the strongest predictors of eventual success is not how talented you appear at the start — it is how far you are from where people thought you would end up. Overestimation, he discovers, is a handicap. Underestimation is a starting position.

The mechanism is what researchers call the "competence calibration gap" — the difference between what people believe about your abilities and what you believe about your abilities. When those numbers are close, complacency tends to follow. You perform at the level you've been told you're capable of. You believe the assessment. You internalize it. If a teacher tells you that you're "not a math person," something in your neural pathways adjusts. Your effort decreases. Your frustration tolerance drops. The prophecy fulfills itself, not through magic but through basic psychological law: belief shapes behavior shapes outcome.

But here is what Grant finds in his research on high performers: the people who achieved the most were often the ones who arrived with a deficit — people who were told they weren't talented, who started behind, who had something to prove. Not everyone with an obstacle overcomes it. But those who do often progress further than people who started with every advantage.

Carol Dweck's growth mindset research is typically used to say "embrace failure." Grant takes it further: what if the real advantage isn't just being able to embrace failure, but having internalized, at a deep level, that you actually need to work harder than the person next to you? Because you were never told you were naturally gifted?

There is a study Grant cites from the psychologist David Dunning showing that when people are genuinely incompetent at something, they lack the metacognitive ability to recognize it. They don't know what they don't know. But people who are aware of their limitations — who know exactly where they're weak — those people tend to work harder around those weaknesses. They develop workarounds. They cultivate allies. They become more creative in their problem-solving because brute force talent is not available to them.

The irony is sharp: being told you are mediocre is, for a certain kind of person, the greatest gift. Because mediocrity creates urgency. Mediocrity creates permission to be experimental. Mediocrity creates the need to prove, which is often the rocket fuel underneath sustained effort.

Grant interviews dozens of people who were told early on that they lacked the requisite talent — and who now outperform people who were always anointed. The pattern is consistent: they developed what might be called "deliberate underperformance psychology." They stopped waiting to feel ready. They stopped measuring themselves against the assumed ceiling. They developed a relationship to their own limitations that transformed limitation into leverage.

A surgeon who was told he didn't have the natural aptitude for surgery, but who developed such meticulous habits that his error rate became the lowest in his institution. A musician who was told she wasn't technically proficient enough to be a professional, but who became obsessive about the specific skills she could control, and eventually became known for a unique style that others couldn't replicate because it required the kind of focus only desperation teaches.

The mechanism here involves something neuroscientists call neuroplasticity — the brain's capacity to reorganize itself in response to experience. But Grant makes a subtle point: neuroplasticity isn't neutral. It responds to motivation. The brain changes most dramatically in response to challenge, but it also changes in response to low expectations. If you expect less from yourself, your neural architecture adapts accordingly. But if you are forced to expect more from yourself — because the world has told you that you're starting with less — your brain mobilizes differently.

There is also a social dimension Grant doesn't ignore. People who are perceived as naturally talented tend to get helped, but they also get watched. Other people become invested in your "potential," which means they're also invested in you maintaining it. You become responsible for not disappointing the image people have constructed. People who are underestimated, by contrast, often receive something more valuable: they are left alone to figure it out.

This autonomy — not because anyone intended to give it to them, but because no one expected much — often converts into creative problem-solving that overestimated people never develop. You can't wait for permission. You can't wait for the right resources. You work with what's available and learn to want nothing you don't have.

Grant proposes a practical reframe: When you are told you're not naturally suited for something, the question isn't "how do I prove them wrong?" (a reactive, honor-based question). The question is: "What do I need to develop, systematically, to become excellent?" That shift from talent-assumption to skill-building is where the trajectory changes.

The other reframe is about estimation itself. We dramatically underestimate how much people grow between 18 and 65. We underestimate how much late bloomers catch up. We underestimate the possibility that the person who was slow to develop in childhood might have been developing in ways we didn't recognize — building patience, learning how to persist, developing an internal compass that doesn't require external validation.

Some of the most accomplished people in their domains were, at the starting line, visibly behind. Not tragically behind. Not in ways that prevented them from trying. But behind in ways that made the label of "talented" unavailable to them. And that unavailability, paradoxically, became their greatest asset.

The hardest part of accepting this is that it requires you to stop waiting to be discovered. If you're underestimated, no one is coming to rescue your potential. You have to do the rescuing yourself. That's uncomfortable. But it's also liberating: your growth isn't dependent on anyone seeing you correctly. It's dependent on you seeing yourself clearly — not as broken, but as unfinished.

---

Today's action: Write down one area where you've been underestimated or where you started behind. Instead of asking "how do I prove them wrong?", ask "what specific skills have I developed because I had to?" Then ask: "What would I develop next, assuming I had to earn this, not inherit it?"

📌 Takeaway: The people who achieve the most are often not the most naturally talented — they are the ones who had to believe their talent was improvable. Underestimation isn't a barrier to success; it's often a prerequisite. It creates the urgency, autonomy, and work ethic that overestimation can't.

💡 Extension: Martin Seligman's learned helplessness research (which you've already explored) shows the dark side: when people learn they can't control outcomes, they stop trying. Grant's work shows the inverse: when people believe they *must* work hard to control outcomes, they often develop capabilities that feel like talent to outside observers. The same neuroscience, applied in opposite directions — one paralyzes, one mobilizes.
