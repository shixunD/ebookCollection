In 2000, the psychologist Sheena Iyengar set up a jam tasting table at a grocery store in Menlo Park, California. On some days, she put out 24 varieties of jam. On others, just 6. The table with 24 jams attracted far more curious visitors — people stopped, looked, tasted. But here is the part nobody forgets: when there were 24 jams available, only 3 percent of shoppers bought anything. When there were just 6, 30 percent bought. Ten times the purchasing rate, with less than a quarter of the options.

This is the paradox Barry Schwartz spent an entire book trying to explain. More choice, he argues, does not make us more free. It makes us more paralyzed, more regretful, and — counterintuitively — less satisfied with whatever we eventually do choose.

We were told the opposite for decades. The received wisdom of consumer capitalism is that options are good, and more options are better. Choice is dignity. Restriction is oppression. The supermarket with 285 varieties of breakfast cereal is, by this logic, a monument to human flourishing. Schwartz's argument, backed by experimental evidence, is that this is precisely wrong — and that the error is not trivial. It is, he suggests, one of the hidden drivers of the modern epidemic of anxiety, regret, and dissatisfaction.

Here is the mechanism. When you choose from two options and the outcome disappoints you, there is not much to regret. You chose the other thing, and maybe it would have been the same anyway. But when you choose from 50 options and the outcome disappoints you, you know with near certainty that one of the 49 paths not taken would have been better. Psychologists call this the opportunity cost of foregone alternatives. Schwartz calls it the cost of regret. The more options you had, the higher that cost. And the anticipation of that cost, before you even choose, is what produces the paralysis.

Not everyone suffers equally from this. Schwartz draws a sharp distinction between two types of decision-makers. Maximizers are people who need to find the best possible option — they read every review, visit every apartment, try every restaurant before committing. Satisficers are people who decide on their criteria in advance and choose the first option that meets them, then stop looking. Common sense says maximizers should do better. They do more research. They are more thorough. And yet study after study shows that maximizers end up less happy with their decisions than satisficers — more anxious before, more regretful after, less able to enjoy what they chose because they are still half-watching the options they passed up.

This is not because maximizers are more neurotic by disposition, though there is some correlation. It is structural. The very behavior that seems most rational — exhaustive search — generates the psychological conditions for maximum regret.

The behavioral economist Daniel Kahneman would recognize this pattern immediately. His work on loss aversion shows that we feel losses roughly twice as intensely as equivalent gains. Every unchosen option, once you have become aware of it, registers as a kind of micro-loss. If you test-drive three cars and buy one, you have technically gained a car. But you have also, psychologically, lost the two you did not pick. Multiply this by every major decision in a modern life — career, city, partner, apartment, career pivot — and the cumulative psychological weight becomes significant.

There is also the phenomenon Schwartz calls adaptation. When you make a choice you cannot reverse, your mind does something elegant: it gradually rationalizes the decision, finds its virtues, makes peace with it. Psychologists call this synthetic happiness, and it is surprisingly durable. But the more reversible a decision is — the more you can return the product, break the lease, change your mind — the less your mind bothers to adapt. Why settle in, when you might leave? The freedom to exit, paradoxically, makes it harder to be happy where you are.

For people in modern knowledge-work careers, this analysis has a specific bite. The career landscape of 2026 is one of almost unlimited optionality — remote work, portfolio careers, constant lateral moves, new fields emerging every three years. This is in many ways genuinely good. But it also means that very few people feel settled. They are always in a state of latent maximizing — always wondering if the next role, the next city, the next industry would have been better. The dissatisfaction is not a symptom of making the wrong choices. It is a symptom of having too many choices and no clear criteria for stopping.

Schwartz's prescription is not to go back to a world with fewer options — that ship has sailed, and nobody would actually want it. His prescription is to act like a satisficer even if you are, by temperament, a maximizer. Decide in advance what good enough looks like. Set your criteria before you start looking, not after. When something meets those criteria, choose it and stop. Do not look back at the full menu. Deliberately constrain your search.

This is, in one sense, a discipline of voluntary blindness. You are choosing not to be aware of options that might, technically, be better. And you are making that choice precisely because full awareness is making you miserable. There is wisdom in this that the optimization culture of the modern world has largely lost. Medieval peasants did not agonize over their career choices. They also did not suffer the specific agony of the person who chose the second-best law firm and spent the next five years reading LinkedIn updates from the person who chose the first.

The Japanese aesthetic concept of wabi-sabi — the beauty of the imperfect and incomplete — gestures at something Schwartz would appreciate. A world in which the chosen thing is valued not for being the best possible thing, but for being the thing that is here, in front of you, that you have decided to invest in. The satisficer's secret is not lower standards. It is the wisdom to know that the search itself has costs, and that at some point, the cost of looking exceeds the value of what you might find.

Here is the micro-action Schwartz's research implies: choose one domain of your life where you are currently in chronic-maximizer mode — the podcast you listen to, the restaurant you order from, the gym you use, the morning routine you follow. Establish your criteria for good enough in that domain. The next time those criteria are met, commit. Do not check what else is available. Notice whether your satisfaction with the choice goes up — not because the thing improved, but because the search ended.

📌 Takeaway: Pick one decision you have been delaying or second-guessing. Write down three criteria that would make a choice good enough. Now look at your current options and pick the first one that qualifies. Stop there. The point is not to find the best option. The point is to experience what happens when you stop looking.

💡 Extension:
- Daniel Kahneman in Thinking, Fast and Slow offers the deeper cognitive mechanics behind why more options generate more regret — through loss aversion and the asymmetric weighting of gains and losses.
- Arthur Brooks in From Strength to Strength makes a parallel argument about life strategy: the people who are most miserable in midlife are often those who maximized every career variable and optimized themselves into an identity crisis.
- Morgan Housel in The Psychology of Money touches on a related idea — that financial contentment often comes not from accumulating the most, but from knowing when enough is enough, and having the discipline to stop before you hit the cliff of diminishing returns.
- Adam Grant in Think Again counters slightly: he argues that we are too quick to commit to our first satisfactory answer in intellectual domains, and that remaining open — a kind of cognitive satisficing with built-in re-evaluation — leads to better thinking. The tension between these views is worth sitting with: satisficing in decisions, but not in beliefs.
