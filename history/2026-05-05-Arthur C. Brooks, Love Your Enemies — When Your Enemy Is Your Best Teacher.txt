When Your Enemy Is Your Best Teacher
Arthur C. Brooks, Love Your Enemies (2024)
Published: 2026-05-05 (Taipei Time)

---

When your enemy becomes stronger, your instinct is to retreat or attack harder. But Arthur Brooks, in Love Your Enemies, proposes something unsettling: your enemy is not your problem. Your enemy is your mirror.

Most of us encounter this idea as a cliché — "you can learn from anyone," "all conflict is growth." We nod. We move on. And we continue avoiding the people who fundamentally disagree with us, because proximity to disagreement feels like proximity to wrongness, and proximity to wrongness feels like a threat to who we are.

Here is what makes Brooks' argument different, and what actually grips the brain: he doesn't ask you to like your enemy. He doesn't ask you to find common ground. He asks you to look at what your anger reveals.

When you are genuinely angry at someone — not irritated, not disappointed, but the kind of anger that loops and returns unbidden — you are experiencing something the psychologists call a value violation. Your enemy has done something or argued something or represented something that directly contradicts a deeply held belief about how the world should work. Your anger is not random. It is information. It is a flare shot from the part of yourself that cares most intensely about something, pointing directly at the thing you care about.

Brooks builds this on research in moral psychology from Jonathan Haidt and others showing that people across the political and ideological spectrum are not motivated by stupidity or malice. They are motivated by different moral foundations — different hierarchies of what matters. A person who values loyalty, authority, and sanctity will see someone who prioritizes fairness, harm prevention, and autonomy as a betrayer. From inside each value system, the other side looks not just wrong but dangerous.

Here is the asymmetry that most conflict resolution misses: when you try to convince your enemy that they're wrong, you are simultaneously telling them that the values they've organized their life around are wrong. You are not asking them to change their mind. You are asking them to disassemble themselves.

No wonder they fight back.

But here is what opens the door: if you can see your enemy's conviction — not agree with it, but see it clearly — you can begin to see what you actually need them to understand. Because underneath every strong disagreement is a value that both people hold. You both want the world to be just. You disagree on what justice looks like. You both want people to be safe. You disagree on what safety requires. You both want to be treated fairly. You disagree on what fairness means.

Brooks calls this "intellectual humility in the service of strength." Not the humility that apologizes or surrenders. The humility that says: I have organized my life around something I believe matters. You have organized your life around something you believe matters. The question is not "which of us is right," but "what does it mean that we both believe our competing things so deeply?"

That shift changes everything. It converts the conversation from a zero-sum game — one of us must be wrong — into a puzzle: how do two people who genuinely disagree on fundamental things still coexist? How do we remain in relationship with people who have chosen different values? What does respect look like when respect doesn't mean agreement?

The research Brooks cites shows something surprising: people are more likely to actually shift their position when they feel truly understood first. Not when they are argued with. Not when the other person finds the fatal logical flaw in their thinking. But when someone takes the time to understand why that position made sense given what they care about. Neuroscientist Marco Iacoboni's work on mirror neurons suggests that when you genuinely try to see the world from someone else's perspective — not to agree with it, but to understand its internal logic — something in your brain changes. You activate the same neural regions they do. You are literally mirroring them.

This is why your enemy can be your best teacher: they are not teaching you that you should believe what they believe. They are teaching you what you actually believe, at depth. They are teaching you what you will argue for. What you will defend. What you cannot compromise on without losing yourself. They are teaching you your own architecture.

There is a famous study where researchers found that people recall information more clearly when it contradicts their existing beliefs than when it confirms them. The brain pays attention to threat. The brain remembers violations. Your enemy, by disagreeing with you in ways that provoke genuine anger, is burning your values into memory with a vividness that agreement never could.

The practical step Brooks suggests is direct: ask your enemy — the person you actually disagree with — "What do you think I don't understand about why you believe what you believe?" Not as a debate tactic. Not as a setup to find the flaw in their reasoning. As a genuine question. Because your enemy, having thought about why they believe something while believing you oppose it, often has clearer access to their own reasoning than you might expect.

What they will likely describe, if they answer honestly, is not stupidity or malice. It is a value you share, weighted differently. A fear that is legitimate. A version of something you care about, configured in a way that makes sense from inside their life.

You will not agree with them. But you will understand them. And in understanding, something shifts. They become less enemy. They become a person. Which does not mean you stop advocating for what you believe. It means you advocate for it differently — not to defeat them, but to understand them while remaining yourself.

---

Today's action: Identify one person you genuinely disagree with on something that matters. Ask them, without sarcasm: "What do you think I don't understand about why you believe what you believe?" Listen to the answer as if you're trying to understand their architecture, not find the flaw in their thinking. Notice what becomes visible about your own values in contrast.

📌 Takeaway: Your enemy is not your problem — your enemy is your mirror. The anger you feel at disagreement is information about what you actually believe. Understanding why someone believes something incompatible with your own beliefs doesn't require agreement; it requires intellectual humility that makes you stronger, not weaker.

💡 Extension: Brené Brown in Daring Greatly explores a related insight — our greatest resistance often points to our greatest vulnerability. Brooks extends this: other people's convictions can become a map of our own unexamined beliefs, if we're willing to look.
