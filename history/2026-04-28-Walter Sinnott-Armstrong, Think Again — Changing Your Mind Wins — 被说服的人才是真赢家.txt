The Person Who Changes Their Mind Always Wins

Think about the last argument you had — a real one, where two people disagreed about something that mattered. Who do you think won?

If you are like most people, you probably said: whoever made the better points and held their position. The one who stayed firm. The one who did not budge.

You have it exactly backwards.

Walter Sinnott-Armstrong, philosopher at Duke University and creator of the Think Again MOOC that attracted over 800,000 students in 150 countries, makes a claim in his book that seems almost offensive the first time you read it: the person who changes their mind during an argument is the real winner. Not the one who held their ground. The one who got convinced.

His logic is uncomfortably airtight. Consider what each person walks away with. The person who did not change their mind ends the conversation in exactly the same position they started in. They may feel satisfied — maybe even victorious — but their understanding of the issue has not moved. They have not gained anything. The person who was convinced, on the other hand, has walked away with something genuinely valuable: an updated model of reality. They now understand something they did not understand before. They corrected a mistake. That is not losing — that is learning.

If you believe that learning is more valuable than winning, then the math is not hard to follow.

The reason this feels counterintuitive is that we have confused two very different games. In a debate competition, the goal is to persuade the audience and defeat the opponent. In that game, changing your mind is indeed a loss. But most of the conversations that actually matter in our lives — with colleagues, partners, children, managers, friends — are not debate competitions. They are attempts to figure out what is true and what to do about it. And in that game, the person who leaves with more accurate beliefs is in the better position, regardless of whether they had to update their views to get there.

Sinnott-Armstrong points to something deeper here about intellectual humility and what he calls the learning function of argument. He argues that one of the most corrosive things about our current cultural moment is that people have stopped treating disagreement as an opportunity to learn and started treating it as a threat to be repelled. We approach arguments in defensive crouch. We come pre-armored. We prepare rebuttals before the other person has finished speaking. We are not listening — we are reloading.

The cost of this posture is enormous. Research on what psychologists call motivated reasoning, the tendency to reason in whatever direction supports our pre-existing beliefs, shows that smart people are not more immune to this bias — they are often more susceptible. Intelligence makes you better at rationalizing what you already believe, not at evaluating it. Daniel Kahneman's research on cognitive bias, and more recently work by Hugo Mercier and Dan Sperber on the argumentative theory of reasoning, suggests that the primary evolutionary function of our reasoning ability may actually have been to win social arguments, not to find truth. We are wired to convince, not to be convinced. Which means deliberately choosing to update your beliefs requires going against a very deep current.

This is why Sinnott-Armstrong's point is not just philosophically interesting — it is behaviorally hard. Choosing to genuinely consider the other person's argument, to look for what is right about it rather than what is wrong, to let your position move if the evidence warrants it — that is an active skill, not a natural one. It is what psychologists call intellectual humility, and it correlates strongly with better decision-making, better relationships, and more genuine influence with others. Paradoxically, the people who are most willing to say I changed my mind tend to be trusted more, not less.

There is a practical dimension to this worth sitting with. How do you actually do it in the middle of a conversation, when you feel the pull to defend yourself?

Sinnott-Armstrong suggests one practice: before you respond to something you disagree with, try to state the other person's argument in its strongest form. Not as you wish they had said it. Not in the version that is easiest to knock down. The strongest version. This is what philosopher Daniel Dennett calls Steelmanning — and it is almost impossible to do without understanding the other person's view well enough that you could defend it yourself. The moment you can do that, two things happen: the conversation changes quality, and your mind becomes genuinely open to the possibility that they are right.

This is harder than it sounds. Most of us Strawman — we grab the weakest interpretation of an opposing view and demolish it, feeling smart all the while. We argue against positions our opponents do not even hold. And we mistake that demolition for victory when we have actually learned nothing and convinced no one who was not already convinced.

None of this means every argument should end in capitulation. Some positions are wrong and should be resisted. Some opponents are not interested in the truth and are simply trying to exhaust you. Sinnott-Armstrong is clear that argument has limits — it works only when both parties are genuinely trying to understand, not simply trying to win. But even in a conversation with someone whose position is ultimately wrong, the willingness to seriously consider it is not weakness. It is what allows you to refute it convincingly rather than simply loudly.

The next time someone changes your mind on something, notice your instinct: does it feel like a defeat? If so, you are playing the wrong game. You just got smarter. That is the whole point.

Today's action: In your next disagreement — whether at work or at home — try this: before responding, spend 60 seconds genuinely articulating the other person's best argument back to them. Say: Let me see if I understand what you're saying, and mean it.

Takeaway: The mark of a great mind is not that it never changes — it is that it changes for the right reasons, and recognizes that as progress rather than defeat.

Extension: Adam Grant in Think Again makes a nearly identical argument — intellectual humility and the willingness to rethink are the defining traits of the most effective thinkers and leaders he has studied.
