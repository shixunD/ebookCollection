The most original thinkers tend to have something unexpected in common: they didn't start as rebels.

This is one of the counterintuitive findings that run through Adam Grant's work on originality. We have a mythology about innovation: the young genius who never fits in, who breaks all the rules from day one, who ignores the gatekeepers and creates something entirely new. Steve Jobs dropping out of college. Mark Zuckerberg building Facebook as a college side project. The archetype is the person who was never comfortable with convention.

But when Grant actually studied how people come up with original ideas, he found something different. Many of the most creative people in history spent years doing "boring" work before their breakthrough. They learned the craft. They understood why the rules existed before they broke them. T.S. Eliot worked as a banker. Greta Garbo was a saleswoman. Benjamin Franklin was an apprentice for years before he did anything that mattered.

Here's the psychological mechanism at work: when you're young and inexperienced, if you try to do something radical, you usually don't have the credibility to get others to take you seriously. Employers reject you. Investors dismiss you. The system filters you out. But if you first prove that you can do the conventional thing well, you build what Grant calls "idiosyncrasy credits." You establish that you understand the game. Then when you try something unconventional, people actually listen.

There's another benefit to starting with convention that's less obvious. When you master something—really master it—you develop deep knowledge of its constraints and possibilities. You understand not just what the rules are, but why they exist. And that's crucial information if you want to break them intelligently. You can see which rules are fundamental and which are outdated. You can spot the leverage points where a small change creates outsized impact.

The reverse often happens to early rebels. They break rules without understanding why the rules were there. So while their work might look novel, it often fails on practical dimensions. They reinvent the wheel because they never learned why wheels are round. Not every rule deserves breaking. Some rules are there because breaking them produces genuinely worse outcomes.

Grant cites studies on creative output: people who have some years of conventional experience but haven't been fully assimilated into the system tend to produce the most original work. They know the craft well enough to be credible and effective. They haven't been there long enough to accept all the implicit assumptions. They're at that sweet spot—informed enough to execute well, but still fresh enough to question everything.

This has real implications for how we develop talent, especially young talent. The current culture pushes people to find their "authentic" voice immediately. Develop your personal brand. Find your passion. Be yourself from day one. But this creates pressure to be original before you've built the foundation to execute original ideas successfully.

There's something valuable in the old apprenticeship model that we've mostly abandoned. You learned the fundamentals. You did the repetitive, unglamorous work. You followed your master's methods precisely until you understood the logic behind them. Only then—often after years—did you start experimenting. By that time, your experiments were informed by deep knowledge.

What does this look like in modern contexts? It might mean that the young software engineer should spend time building "boring" features well before attempting to redesign the entire system. The junior marketer should understand how campaigns actually convert customers before trying to completely reinvent the funnel. The new manager should focus on becoming genuinely good at the standard practices before introducing novel ones.

One reason this matters is that being bad at execution kills originality. A brilliant idea executed poorly is just a failed project. An ordinary idea executed exceptionally well often becomes successful and then gets refined into something truly innovative. The execution carries the idea forward.

There's also a social element Grant keeps emphasizing. When you come in as a young person with radical ideas and you're bad at the basics, people dismiss the radical ideas as symptoms of your inexperience. "Oh, you don't understand how the real world works." But if you come in, master the basics, prove you can play the game—and then suggest a radical alternative—people have to take you seriously. You've demonstrated competence. Now your unconventional ideas seem like informed critiques rather than naïve idealism.

The tricky part is not losing the unconventional thinking in the process of learning convention. Some people get so good at following the existing system that they lose the capacity to question it. They become true believers. Grant's research suggests the people who avoid this are those who maintain some psychological distance—they do the work excellently, but they don't fully internalize the system's logic as truth.

One way to maintain that distance is to stay curious about the underlying assumptions. While you're learning the conventional way, keep asking "why?" Why do we do it this way? What would happen if we did it differently? What are we optimizing for, and are those the right things to optimize for? You're conforming to the system while simultaneously analyzing it. That position—inside but not assimilated—is where genuine innovation typically originates.

📌 Takeaway: If you're early in your career or working in a new domain, resist the pressure to be original immediately. Instead, focus completely on becoming genuinely good at the fundamentals. Once you can execute the standard approach better than most people, then start experimenting with unconventional approaches. You'll have both the credibility and the knowledge to do it effectively.

💡 Extension: Arthur Brooks touches on a related idea in Build the Life You Want—that the capacity for reinvention comes not from constant novelty-seeking, but from deep knowledge that lets you see new applications. Carol Dweck's growth mindset research (which Grant builds on) shows that people who first master a skill and then seek challenges in it make more progress than people who chase novelty without foundation. The pattern across all these researchers is consistent: mastery precedes authentic innovation.
