Pull out your phone and open your calculator. Type in a number: how many times per year do you see the person you love most — a parent, a sibling, an old friend who lives in another city. Now subtract their current age from eighty. Multiply those two numbers together.

That is, roughly, how many times you will see them before they are gone.

For most people who do this exercise, the number is somewhere between eight and fifty. Not thousands. Not hundreds. Dozens. Maybe less. And the shocking part is not just the number itself — it is that you already knew, in some abstract sense, that time was finite. You have always known. And yet the number still lands like a diagnosis.

Sahil Bloom calls this the Time Wealth Hard Reset in The 5 Types of Wealth. The exercise is almost violently simple. And that simplicity is the point. We are extraordinarily good at holding abstract truths — "life is short," "people don't last forever," "I should call more" — in a part of our minds that never touches behavior. The math does something different. It makes the abstract arithmetically precise. It removes the comfortable vagueness that lets us keep postponing.

Bloom shares the story of Dave Prout, a video game executive who came across a post about finite visits while his mother was undergoing cancer treatment in Seattle. Dave lived three thousand miles away and was seeing his parents roughly twice a year. When he did the math — his mother's age, the approximate years remaining, the frequency of visits — the number he arrived at was small enough to change his behavior immediately. He began flying to Seattle every six weeks. He accumulated what he described as a "whole set of memories" he would not otherwise have had. His mother passed away in May 2023. He was there.

What strikes me about this story is not that the math revealed something Dave didn't know. He knew his mother was ill. He knew distance was a factor. He knew time was running out. He knew all of it. And he was still seeing her twice a year. The math didn't add information — it removed the psychological distance between knowing and acting.

Psychologists call this the intention-action gap. We hold intentions that never become behaviors, not because we don't mean them, but because the cognitive machinery that generates behavior requires something more visceral than abstract belief. It requires what researcher Peter Gollwitzer calls an "implementation intention" — a specific, concrete, emotional prompt that closes the loop between wanting to do something and actually doing it. The finite visits math is exactly this kind of prompt. It converts "I should visit more" into "I will see my father seventeen more times in my life unless I change something."

There is a broader principle here about how we relate to time. The philosopher William James wrote that the greatest revolution of his generation was the discovery that human beings can alter their lives by altering their attitudes. But attitudes, it turns out, are not changed by arguments. They are changed by vivid experiences — real or imagined — that make the future feel real and near rather than abstract and distant.

We are, in the language of behavioral economics, deeply hyperbolic discounters. We systematically undervalue future experiences relative to present ones. Not because we are selfish or shallow, but because the brain's reward circuitry responds to what is in front of us, not to what is coming later. The distant parent visit competes every week with the immediate pull of work, routines, inertia, and the low-grade exhaustion of a full life. And in that competition, the immediate almost always wins — not because it matters more, but because it is louder.

The finite visits math hacks this. By making the future feel like a finite inventory rather than an open horizon, it creates urgency from arithmetic alone. And urgency is what moves the needle.

This insight extends well beyond family. Think about how you relate to your friendships. Research by Robin Dunbar, the British anthropologist famous for "Dunbar's Number," found that close friendships require roughly 200 hours of shared time per year to maintain their depth. Below that threshold, the friendship slowly recedes — not through conflict or rupture, but through quiet drift. Most people, once they leave school or a shared workplace, see their closest friends between two and six times a year. Run that math. The friendship you think of as deep and lasting may be quietly fading simply because the arithmetic no longer supports it.

Or think about your own life. How many summers do you have left in which you are healthy enough to hike the trail you keep meaning to hike? How many years does your child have before the window of genuine curiosity — when they still ask you questions and want your answer — closes? How many seasons of the garden, how many morning runs while your knees still cooperate, how many conversations with a mentor who is getting older? None of these are morbid questions. They are calibration questions. The point is not to be haunted by finitude but to let finitude do what it does best: clarify.

Graham Duncan, an investor and co-founder of East Rock Capital, coined the term "time billionaire" for people who have over a billion seconds left in their lives — essentially anyone under fifty. His observation was that the young analysts he interviewed year after year all believed, at some level, that their happiness was one financial milestone away. But he noticed that Warren Buffett — ninety-four years old, with access to anyone in the world — would almost certainly trade his net worth for a young analyst's remaining seconds. The paradox is that we are most wasteful with time precisely when we have the most of it.

The finite visits math is not a tool for anxiety. It is a tool for attention. It does not tell you that time is running out in a way that should frighten you into paralysis. It tells you that time is running out in a way that should help you decide: what actually deserves a spot in that shrinking inventory?

The answer is probably not the extra meeting. Probably not the project you took on to seem impressive. Probably not the passive scroll through someone else's highlight reel. The answer, for most people, is a version of what Dave Prout discovered after the math: that the people you love are worth the flight, the rearranged schedule, the slightly inconvenient Tuesday. Because there are fewer Tuesdays than you think.

📌 Takeaway: Do the math today. Pick one person you love and don't see enough. Multiply the times per year you see them by the approximate years remaining. Write the number down. Then book the next visit before you close this page.

💡 Extension: Morgan Housel in Same as Ever writes that good news compounds slowly and invisibly while bad news arrives instantly — a parallel insight: the gradual drift away from people we love is invisible until it isn't, while the ruptures that make headlines are immediate. Robert Waldinger, in the Harvard Study of Adult Development (covered April 24), found that relationship quality at 50 predicts physical health at 80 — the finite visits math is, in this sense, not just emotional but literally biological. Bronnie Ware, the palliative care nurse whose research became The Top Five Regrets of the Dying, found that the most common regret was not working less, but losing touch with friends — the people whose visit-count quietly fell to zero.
