The CV of Failure

Here is a resume exercise that will make most high achievers deeply uncomfortable: write down every job you did not get, every project that collapsed, every relationship that fell apart, every grant you were denied. Do not list what you learned. Just list what failed. Stare at it.

If that feels unbearable, you are not alone — and that discomfort is precisely the point.

Arthur C. Brooks, behavioral scientist and Harvard professor, argues in The Happiness Files that one of the most powerful tools for professional resilience is something almost nobody does: maintaining a written record of your failures. Not a private journal of regrets, not a self-help exercise in finding the silver lining, but a real, dated list of things you tried and lost. He draws this idea from a 2010 article in Nature by Melanie Stefan, who proposed that academics publish CVs of failure alongside their regular CVs — to normalize rejection and make the path to success legible.

Most of us do the opposite. We carefully curate. LinkedIn is a highlight reel. Our bios describe the arc of triumph. The messy middle — the year we got fired, the startup that died, the book that never found a publisher — gets quietly buried. We become, in a sense, unreliable narrators of our own lives.

Brooks cites a striking study by psychologist Xiaodong Lin at Columbia University, who researched how students respond to learning about famous scientists. One group learned about scientists' achievements only. Another group learned about both their achievements and their failures. The students who studied the failures — Einstein's rejected papers, Curie's battles for acceptance, Edison's thousands of non-working experiments — performed significantly better in their own academic work. Not because failure is inspiring in some vague motivational sense, but because it makes the path to success realistic. When you understand that the people you admire also failed constantly, failure stops feeling like evidence that you do not belong.

This matters more than it seems. The reason failure is so crushing is not the failure itself — it is what we make of it. Brooks points out that when we ruminate on failure in our heads, we keep it in the murky realm of emotions, where it morphs and magnifies. Rolling a failure around in your mind is not reflection — it is amplification. The failure gets larger, more defining, more permanent than it actually is. You failed to get the promotion. Now your mind whispers: No one will ever hire you. Your career is over. You were never as good as you thought. This is what psychologists call catastrophizing — and it is almost entirely a product of keeping the failure inside.

Writing the failure down changes the cognitive process. It forces your prefrontal cortex into the conversation. Once the failure is on the page, it has edges. It has a date. It has a specific, bounded shape. It cannot sprawl into everything. Researcher Xiaodong Lin calls this the difference between emotional processing and cognitive processing — and the latter is considerably more useful for learning.

Brooks suggests something practical alongside the list: next to each failure, add a line for lesson learned. Not in a forced, toxic-positivity way — sometimes the lesson is simply I misjudged the situation or I was not ready yet. But the act of naming the lesson closes the loop. It converts the failure from a judgment about your identity into a data point about the world.

There is a deeper reason to do this work, though — one that goes beyond resilience psychology. It has to do with how we relate to other people who are failing. When we hide our own failures, we implicitly signal to those around us that failure is shameful. We inadvertently build cultures — in families, teams, and organizations — where people are afraid to take risks, afraid to try things that might not work, afraid to admit when they are stuck. The leader who has never spoken openly about a professional failure is not seen as strong. They are seen as either lucky or dishonest. And neither builds the kind of trust that makes a team willing to attempt hard things.

Thomas Edison's most famous quote gets repeated so often it has become a cliche: I have not failed. I've just found ten thousand ways that won't work. But what gets missed is that Edison actually documented his failures. He kept meticulous notebooks. He treated every failed experiment not as evidence of incompetence, but as evidence of the system working. The failure was the data. The CV was real.

You will fail. Not occasionally — regularly, across the span of a life spent trying things. The question is not whether your failures will accumulate but whether you will account for them honestly, learn from them specifically, and eventually let them make you more realistic about what it actually takes to do something hard. The most dangerous thing about a curated success narrative is not the dishonesty — it is the loneliness it creates. You walk around comparing your honest interior to everyone else's polished exterior, and you conclude that you are the only one struggling.

You are not. Start the list.

Today's action: Open a document right now and write down three professional failures from the past five years — include the date and one honest sentence about what you learned or did not learn. Save it as Failures.doc and revisit it quarterly.

Takeaway: Failure only destroys you when it lives inside your head. Write it down, give it a date, and it becomes data instead of identity.

Extension: Adam Grant in Hidden Potential argues similarly — discomfort and imperfection are the raw material of growth, not detours around it.
