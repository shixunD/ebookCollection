Admitting What You Don't Know Is the Mark of Competence

There's a moment in most expert interviews where something reverses. You ask the genuine master—the surgeon, the artist, the scientist—what they still don't understand about their field, and suddenly their whole demeanor changes. They light up. They stop performing certainty and start thinking. They become, in that moment, more impressive than they were before.

Most people get this backwards. We think certainty is the mark of competence. We assume the person who never says "I don't know" is the person most worth listening to. So we hide our uncertainty. We cover the gaps. We perform confidence even when we feel doubt. And in doing so, we signal to everyone around us that not knowing is a defect, a weakness to be hidden—not a fact of how actual knowledge works.

Arthur C. Brooks, in Love Your Enemies, calls this "intellectual humility," and he argues it's not a personality trait—it's a skill, and like most skills, it's strongest in people with the deepest actual expertise.

The psychological research here is clean. In a series of studies on overconfidence, researchers have found a consistent pattern: the more someone knows about a topic, the more comfortable they are admitting what they don't know about it. Conversely, the Dunning-Kruger effect (named for the work of David Dunning and Justin Kruger) shows that people with shallow knowledge tend toward massive overconfidence. They don't know enough to know what they don't know. They fill the gaps with conviction. They sound certain because they've never encountered enough complexity to realize how much is uncertain.

The truly knowledgeable person has hit the wall of what nobody knows. They've studied long enough to realize that the frontier of knowledge in any field is fuzzy and contested and that confident pronouncements often come from people who haven't looked closely enough at the edge cases. They've learned to be comfortable saying "I think that's true, but I could be wrong, and here's why I might be wrong."

This sounds like weakness. It's not. It's the opposite.

Consider a surgeon. The surgeon who tells you, "I've done this procedure 200 times, and in your case, here's what I think is happening, but I want to run one more test because I'm seeing something that doesn't fit my expectation," is more trustworthy than the surgeon who says, "I know exactly what this is." The first surgeon is actually describing expertise—the ability to recognize when a situation is more complex than the standard protocol. The second surgeon is describing either a genuine master (rare) or someone who's performing competence rather than possessing it.

Brooks argues that intellectual humility isn't about self-doubt. It's about intellectual precision. It's the recognition that confident statements should be reserved for things you've actually thought through, and that the boundaries of your certainty are places worth describing clearly.

But here's where it gets interesting for your actual life: intellectual humility isn't just epistemically correct—it changes how other people respond to you.

Think about the people you actually trust when it matters. If you're making a big decision—a career move, a medical choice, a financial bet—you probably don't want to hear from the person who is absolutely certain. You want to hear from someone who clearly understands the stakes, has thought about the various possibilities, and can tell you which variables matter most and which ones are still uncertain. That person sounds less impressive in a casual conversation. They sound more impressive in a moment that actually matters.

There's a study by researcher Anna Salter on interpersonal trust that found something striking: people trust statements more when the speaker acknowledges limits and caveats than when they offer unqualified certainty. Not because people are drawn to uncertainty, but because acknowledging uncertainty is a signal that someone is trying to be accurate rather than impressive. It signals that you're not trying to fool yourself or them.

This reverses how most of us manage information in social and professional contexts. We think we gain status by sounding like we know more than we do. We think expressing doubt makes us look less competent. The research suggests the opposite: admitting uncertainty to the people who matter most creates trust, and trust is a far more valuable form of status than the feeling of having impressed someone with false certainty.

But getting there requires something that's genuinely hard: tolerating the feeling that you look less impressive in the moment in exchange for being more trustworthy over time. You have to be willing to be the person in the meeting who says, "That's an interesting argument, but I need to think about it more before I'm sure," instead of the person with the snappy comeback and the false confidence. You have to be okay with sitting with complexity longer than the person next to you.

Brooks describes intellectual humility as a form of moral courage. Because it's easier to be certain. Certainty feels good. Admitting what you don't know—especially in competitive or hierarchical contexts—feels like volunteering to be underestimated. And often, in the moment, it does result in underestimation. But over time, it's the opposite. The person who can say clearly what they know and don't know is the one people actually follow when things get hard.

One caveat that Brooks emphasizes: intellectual humility is not the same as never taking a stand. You can hold a position strongly and still acknowledge what you don't know about it. "I think this approach is right, and here's my reasoning. I could be wrong about X, Y, Z. But based on what I know, I'm committed to trying it" is not wishy-washy. It's precise. It's humble without being paralyzed. It's the opposite of the false confidence that says "I'm certain" when what it really means is "I'm too insecure to admit uncertainty."

The specific skill Brooks says to practice: whenever you find yourself making a strong statement, ask—what would have to be true for me to be wrong about this? Can you actually articulate it? If you can't, you're probably not thinking clearly. If you can, you've just turned intellectual humility into intellectual precision. You've moved from performing certainty to actually understanding something.

This changes conversations. It changes how people listen to you. It changes what you're capable of learning from the person across from you—including people you disagree with, because you're no longer defending a position so rigidly that you can't afford to hear critique.

The strongest people you know probably do this. They probably say "I don't know" more easily than the people around them. Not because they're less confident. Because they're confident enough to be accurate.

---

Today's action: In your next meeting or conversation with someone whose opinion you respect, pay attention to how they handle uncertainty. Do they hedge? Do they qualify? Do they acknowledge what they don't know? Then notice: does their willingness to express uncertainty make you trust them less or more? Spend one day listening for intellectual humility instead of certainty, and see what you learn about who you actually trust.

Takeaway: Intellectual humility isn't weakness—it's the hallmark of actual expertise. The people worth listening to are comfortable saying "I don't know" because they know enough to recognize the limits of knowing.

Extension: Adam Grant in Think Again explores a similar phenomenon in the context of changing your mind—people most willing to admit they were wrong are often the ones with the deepest confidence in their ability to get it right eventually. Also, Arthur C. Brooks in Strength to Strength touches on a related idea: that pursuing mastery requires moving beyond the need to look masterful, which means tolerating looking uncertain in the intermediate stages. The research on learning suggests intellectual humility and the capacity to learn are inseparable.
