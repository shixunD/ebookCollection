When you feel sad, you're taught to fix it. Not understand it. Your therapist wants you to feel better. Your friends offer solutions. Your productivity app reminds you that sadness is a "downtime" to minimize. This is the modern error: we've medicalized sorrow and forgotten it might be the healthiest thing you're feeling right now.

Susan David, a psychologist who spent years interviewing people about their lives' turning points, noticed something her training hadn't prepared her for. The people who reported deepest meaning—not happiness, meaning—were often the ones who'd learned to live alongside their sadness rather than against it. Not romanticizing depression. Not wallowing. But recognizing that sorrow and meaning are often woven together so tightly you can't have one without the other.

Here's the counterintuitive part: trying to be relentlessly happy might be the thing preventing you from building a life worth living.

David calls this quality "bittersweet"—the ability to hold joy and sorrow in the same moment without needing to choose. We think of bittersweet as a flavor, but it's really a way of being. It's the parent who feels relief when their adult child finally moves out, and devastation about the loss of daily presence. It's the person who's achieved their decade-long goal and feels empty because the striving is gone. It's the grief of getting what you wanted, because getting it means something else is over.

The problem is we've created a culture that doesn't have space for this feeling. We've partitioned life into "good things that make you happy" and "bad things that make you sad," and we treat sad as a problem to solve rather than a signal to honor. But research in emotion science shows something more complex: the same neural circuits that generate sorrow also generate meaning-making. When you feel sadness, you're not just feeling hurt—you're activating the part of your brain that's asking "What does this matter?" and "How does this change what I understand about what's valuable?"

David's research with people who've experienced loss—bereavement, divorce, career disruption—found something striking. The ones who tried hardest to "get over it" and "move on" often reported feeling hollow years later. They'd reached their happiness target, but the joy felt thin, cardboard. By contrast, those who allowed themselves to stay sad *while still building meaningful things* reported richer lives. Not sadder lives. Richer.

This has implications for how you're living right now. If there's something you've been trying to stop feeling sad about—a career shift that felt like failure even though it was right, a relationship that ended despite your love, an opportunity missed—the cultural message is clear: don't let this define you, move on, find the silver lining. And sometimes those are good suggestions. But sometimes the best thing you can do is this: let it be sad. Let it be significant. Let it shape what comes next.

The bittersweet person doesn't fight sorrow; they let it teach them about what matters. And strangely, that's often when joy becomes more real.

📌 **Takeaway:** Today, name one sadness you've been trying to "fix" and ask instead: What is this sorrow trying to tell me about what I value? Sit with that for 15 minutes without solving anything.

💡 **Extension:** Morgan Housel touches on similar terrain in "Same as Ever"—the idea that perfection is actually a poor strategy, and that losses/sadness are features of a well-lived life, not bugs. Brené Brown explores related ideas in "Daring Greatly" about how we numb pain and miss out on depth. Viktor Frankl would recognize in this the search for meaning even (especially) through suffering.
