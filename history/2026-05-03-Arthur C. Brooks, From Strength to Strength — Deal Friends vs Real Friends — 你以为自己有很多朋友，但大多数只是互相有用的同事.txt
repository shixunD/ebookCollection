Arthur C. Brooks, From Strength to Strength — Deal Friends vs Real Friends — 你以为自己有很多朋友，但大多数只是互相有用的同事

---

Most people who reach midlife with full calendars and a long contact list are, if they are honest, profoundly lonely.

Not lonely in the melodramatic sense. Lonely in the specific, quiet way that only becomes visible at 2am when something goes badly wrong and you scroll through your phone wondering: who can I actually call?

Arthur C. Brooks has a precise diagnosis for this. In From Strength to Strength, he draws a distinction that most productivity culture actively suppresses: the difference between deal friends (利益型朋友) and real friends (真实型朋友).

---

Deal friends are not bad people. They are, by the normal standards of the word, your "friends." You grab coffee, you refer business, you celebrate each other's wins on LinkedIn. The relationship has genuine warmth. But underneath that warmth is a load-bearing structure made of mutual utility (相互效用): they are useful to you, you are useful to them, and the connection persists precisely because that equation balances.

Brooks cites Aristotle here — specifically the Nicomachean Ethics, where Aristotle distinguishes three types of friendship: friendships of utility (功利型), friendships of pleasure (愉悦型), and friendships of virtue (美德型). Only the third kind, Aristotle argued, is complete friendship. The other two are friendships of convenience that dissolve when the convenience does.

Nearly two and a half millennia later, Brooks finds Aristotle's taxonomy uncomfortably accurate. The higher you climb professionally, he observes, the more your social world fills with utility-friendships — and the harder it becomes to notice that the deeper kind is disappearing.

---

The data on this is not encouraging.

A 2021 survey by the Survey Center on American Life found that 15% of American men reported having no close friends — up from 3% in 1990. For women, the numbers were better but trending in the same direction. Simultaneously, professional networks (职业社交网络) had never been larger or more connected. More LinkedIn connections. More conference contacts. More Slack channels with people you see twice a year in person.

We have more deal friends than any generation in history. And fewer real ones.

Brooks argues this is not coincidental. Professional success creates a gravitational field that attracts deal-friendship and quietly crowds out real-friendship. Real friends require something that deal friends do not: time with no agenda, vulnerability (脆弱), and the willingness to be known in your unperformed state. High-achieving people, trained to optimize every interaction for outcome, find this deeply uncomfortable. So they fill their calendars with useful dinners instead.

---

This has a specific cost that shows up later than most people expect.

The Harvard Study of Adult Development — the longest-running study of human happiness in existence, tracking men for over 80 years — found that the single strongest predictor of health and happiness in late life was not wealth, not achievement, not even physical fitness. It was the quality of close relationships. Not the quantity. Not the professional prestige of your network. The depth.

Brooks connects this directly to the second-curve problem he writes about throughout the book. The first curve of professional life rewards performance, output, and status — precisely the things deal-friendships maintain. The second curve — the one that sustains you after peak professional achievement — runs entirely on the kind of connection you cannot manufacture at a networking dinner.

If you spent the first curve collecting deal friends and neglecting real ones, you arrive at the second curve with an impressive network and a hollow foundation.

---

The question Brooks poses is not "do you have friends." Almost everyone answers yes to that.

The real question is: how many people in your life know what you are actually afraid of? How many have seen you fail, stayed anyway, and would describe you accurately — including the unflattering parts — to a stranger?

If you are counting carefully, the number is probably smaller than your contact list suggests.

This is not a reason for despair. Brooks is ultimately an optimist about human capacity for connection. But he is clear that real friendship requires a decision — an active choice to invest time and vulnerability in a small number of people, rather than perpetually expanding the radius of your useful acquaintances.

Morgan Housel, writing about money, reaches an adjacent truth: the most valuable things in life — independence, time, love — are almost never the things we are most publicly seen to be acquiring. We optimize loudly for the wrong currency, and the compound interest on that mistake accumulates quietly for decades.

Deal friends are a feature of the first curve. Real friends are what make the second curve survivable.

Start auditing early.
