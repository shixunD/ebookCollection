Your Worry Is Not the Problem, Your Relationship with Worry Is
Arthur C. Brooks, The Happiness Files (2024)
Published: 2026-05-05 (Taipei Time)

---

Here is a question that might stop you mid-sip of your morning coffee: What if eliminating your worries is the wrong goal entirely?

Most advice about anxiety goes like this — identify the source, fix it, move on. If you are worried about money, make more. If you are worried about a health issue, see a doctor. If you are worried about a relationship, have the conversation. The implicit assumption is that worry is a problem to be solved, and once the problem disappears, the worry disappears with it. It feels logical. It feels adult. It is, for most of us, completely wrong.

Arthur Brooks, behavioral scientist and Harvard professor, draws a sharp distinction in The Happiness Files between what worry is and what we think it is. Worry, he writes, is a recursive mental attempt to resolve a situation with an uncertain, possibly negative outcome. Notice what that definition includes: uncertain outcomes. Not bad outcomes. Not even likely-bad outcomes. Merely uncertain ones. Which means worry does not require a real threat — it only requires the absence of a guarantee. And since no one in the history of human civilization has ever received a guarantee about the future, we are, structurally, all set up to be chronic worriers.

This is the first uncomfortable truth: worry is not a signal that something is wrong. It is a signal that something is unknown. And in a world of infinite unknowns, trying to eliminate worry by eliminating uncertainty is like trying to dry yourself off while standing in the rain.

The second uncomfortable truth is even sharper. Research from multiple psychiatric journals that Brooks cites in the book shows that the very act of trying to suppress worry makes it worse. Psychologist Daniel Wegner's famous "white bear" experiments demonstrated that instructing people not to think about something causes them to think about it more frequently — a phenomenon called the rebound effect (反弹效应). When you tell your anxious brain "stop worrying about this," what the brain actually hears is "load this topic into working memory so we can monitor it constantly." The suppression strategy backfires every single time.

What actually works is both counterintuitive and deceptively simple: write it down. Not to solve it. Not to make a plan. Just to externalize it.

Brooks points to the work of psychologist James Pennebaker, whose decades of research on expressive writing show that putting anxious thoughts on paper moves them from the murky, emotion-dominated limbic system into the prefrontal cortex — the part of your brain that does actual reasoning (认知处理). When worry lives only in your head, it stays in an ambiguous, threatening fog. When it lives on paper, the brain reclassifies it from "active threat" to "considered item." It does not go away — but it loses its grip.

The mechanism here is what neuroscientists call affect labeling (情绪标注): the process of naming and describing an emotion in concrete language measurably reduces the activity of the amygdala, the brain's fear center. A 2007 study by Matthew Lieberman at UCLA showed that participants who labeled their emotional experiences had significantly lower amygdala activation than those who simply experienced the same emotions without naming them. Worry on paper is worry with its teeth pulled.

But Brooks pushes even further, and this is where his thinking gets genuinely disruptive. He argues that some worry is not a flaw to fix — it is actually information to listen to. He distinguishes between useful worry (anxiety that points you toward a specific, actionable step) and useless worry (anxiety that loops endlessly around scenarios you cannot influence). The goal is not zero worry. The goal is routing your worry budget only toward the first category.

This matters especially for high-achievers, who tend to treat worry as a fuel source. The logic goes: if I stop worrying about this project, I will get complacent, something will go wrong, and it will be my fault. This is the catastrophic belief that anxiety is somehow necessary for performance. But the research says otherwise. A meta-analysis in the Journal of Anxiety Disorders found that chronic worrying correlates with worse problem-solving — not better — because worry consumes working memory capacity that you actually need to think clearly.

There is a famous anecdote about Winston Churchill, who once said he had experienced many terrible things in his life, most of which never happened. Churchill understood intuitively what the science now confirms: the cost of worry is paid upfront, in full, regardless of whether the feared outcome ever arrives. You lose the sleep. You lose the focus. You lose the time. And then, in most cases, the thing you feared simply does not happen. You paid the full price for nothing.

Brooks' prescription is elegant in its simplicity. Each morning, instead of trying to push your worries away, write them down in one column. In a second column, write whether there is one specific action you can take in the next 24 hours. If yes, take it and move on. If no, close the notebook. You have officially done everything worrying can do for you. The rest is just noise.

This is not toxic positivity. It is not pretending the world is fine. It is, in fact, a higher form of realism — one that acknowledges that your worry and the future are operating on entirely different schedules, and only one of them is worth your time today.

Today's action: Tonight before bed, write down your top three current worries. For each one, write: "Is there one specific action I can take in the next 24 hours?" If yes, schedule it. If no, physically close the notebook. Notice whether the act of writing changes the weight of what you're carrying.

📌 Takeaway: Worry is not a signal that something is wrong — it is a signal that something is unknown. Writing it down does not solve it, but it reclassifies it from "active threat" to "considered item," reducing your amygdala's grip and giving your prefrontal cortex a chance to actually help.

💡 Extension: Daniel Wegner in White Bears and Other Unwanted Thoughts offers a similar finding — the harder you try to suppress a thought, the more it rebounds; his research directly supports Brooks' case against worry-suppression as a strategy.
