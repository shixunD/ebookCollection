Nobody warns you that freedom can be taken from you in layers.

First your job, then your savings, then your health, then the people you love. Viktor Frankl lost all of it — in that order, and then some. By 1944 he was a prisoner inside Auschwitz, his manuscript confiscated, his wife taken to a separate camp where she would later die, his parents already gone. He owned nothing. He controlled nothing. He could not choose what the guards did to him before breakfast.

And yet he noticed something that would eventually become one of the most radical ideas in 20th-century psychology: between what happens to you and how you respond to it, there is always a gap. And inside that gap lives the last freedom nobody can take — the freedom to choose your attitude.

This sounds, at first pass, like the kind of thing you'd cross-stitch on a pillow. It is not.

Frankl was a psychiatrist before the war. He had spent years studying what kept people psychologically alive under pressure. Inside the camps, he became a ruthless empiricist of human suffering, watching which prisoners survived not just physically but mentally. He noticed it was rarely the strongest ones. It was the ones who could find — or construct — a reason to keep going. The ones who had something to live for: a child they imagined seeing again, a book they planned to finish, a person they refused to let down. Meaning, he concluded, is not discovered. It is made. And the making of it is the one act of authorship that no external force can fully revoke.

The psychologist Edith Eva Eger, herself a Holocaust survivor and later a therapist, describes it similarly in her memoir The Choice. She writes that the Nazis could imprison her body, but they could not imprison her mind unless she let them. She survived partly by dancing ballet in her imagination during roll calls. The external world was a prison. The internal world was still, in some crucial way, hers.

This is not toxic positivity. Frankl was ferociously clear on this point. He did not say suffering is good, or that you should smile through pain, or that everything happens for a reason. He said something far more specific: that suffering is inevitable, but whether suffering destroys you or becomes the raw material of something meaningful is, to a remarkable degree, up to you. The German word he used was Trotzdem — meaning nevertheless, or in spite of everything. You can say yes to life in spite of everything.

The behavioral science behind this is now substantial. Psychologists call it response flexibility — the ability to pause between stimulus and response, to interrupt the automatic chain of reaction. Research by James Gross at Stanford on emotion regulation shows that people who practice cognitive reappraisal — that is, consciously reconsidering the meaning of what they experience — report higher life satisfaction, lower rates of depression, and better physiological stress recovery. The body literally calms down faster when the mind has found a frame.

But here is what I think most people miss about Frankl: he was not talking about optimism. He was not saying that if you think positive thoughts, good things will happen. He was saying something structurally different — that meaning can coexist with pain, and that the search for meaning is itself stabilizing, even before you find it. The act of asking what this experience might be for has a different emotional signature than the act of asking why this is happening to me. One is generative. The other is a loop.

There is an experiment I keep returning to. In a 1998 study, psychologist Crystal Park found that people who experienced serious negative life events — illness, loss, failure — but were able to construct a coherent narrative around them recovered not just faster, but to a higher psychological baseline than before the event. Researchers call this post-traumatic growth. It is not universal, and it is not automatic. But it is far more common than our culture acknowledges. The people who achieved it were not the ones who denied the difficulty. They were the ones who eventually made something of it.

Frankl had a word for what he was prescribing: logotherapy, from the Greek logos, meaning meaning. His basic clinical claim was that the deepest human motivation is not pleasure, not power, but the will to meaning. When that will is frustrated — when life feels pointless — people collapse into what he called an existential vacuum. This vacuum, he argued, explains a huge range of modern pathologies: depression, aggression, addiction, the strange boredom of people who have everything but feel nothing.

If you look at the data on meaning in modern life, the picture is alarming. A 2023 Gallup survey found that nearly a quarter of Americans say they rarely or never feel their lives have clear direction or meaning. This number is highest among young adults. We are not, most of us, in a concentration camp. We are in the suburbs, with delivery apps and decent internet and more choices than any generation in history. And yet something is hollowed out.

Frankl would not be surprised. His argument was that comfort and meaning are not the same thing, and that a life organized primarily around the elimination of discomfort is a life that will accidentally eliminate much of its own meaning. Suffering, when it cannot be avoided, needs to be metabolized, not just managed. And metabolism requires a story — some account of why this matters, who it is making you, what you are moving toward.

You do not have to be in extremis to practice this. The gap between stimulus and response exists in ordinary moments too. In the conversation that goes badly. In the project that fails. In the diagnosis that changes everything. In the relationship that falls apart. In each of these, there is a moment — sometimes very brief — where you choose the frame before the frame chooses you.

Frankl called this the defiant power of the human spirit. I love that phrase. Not passive acceptance. Not forced positivity. Defiance. A stubborn insistence that you will be the author of your own meaning, even when you cannot be the author of your own circumstances.

Today is a good day to practice on something small.

📌 Takeaway: Before you react to something frustrating today — a rejection, a setback, an interruption — take ten seconds to ask not why it happened, but what you want it to mean about who you are and where you are going. That ten seconds is Frankl's gap. Use it.

💡 Extension:
- Morgan Housel in Same as Ever makes a related point about the psychology of adversity — that the people who thrive long-term are not those who avoid hard things but those who carry them without being defined by them.
- Adam Grant in Option B (co-authored with Sheryl Sandberg) explores post-traumatic growth in detail — how resilience is built not by denial but by finding what Frankl would recognize as meaning in loss.
- Arthur Brooks in From Strength to Strength touches on the opposite failure mode: people who, when their fluid intelligence peaks and declines, interpret it as pure loss rather than transition — because they have no meaning framework outside their professional identity.
- Brene Brown in Daring Greatly offers a contrasting but complementary view: she argues that the willingness to be vulnerable — to acknowledge that something hurt — is a prerequisite for the kind of meaning-making Frankl describes. You cannot construct meaning around pain you are busy pretending does not exist.
