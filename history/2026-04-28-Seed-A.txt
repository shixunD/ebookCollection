The Missionary Problem Nobody Talks About

You probably think you're good at changing people's minds. Someone brings up a topic you disagree with—politics, parenting, money—and you pull out your best evidence, your most logical arguments, your most compelling story. You're convinced that if they just understand your perspective, they'll shift. And when they don't? You assume they didn't listen carefully enough, or they're too stubborn, or they lack the information you possess.

Here's what the research actually shows: You're almost certainly overestimating your persuasive power.

Adam Grant studied this phenomenon across corporate settings, political discussions, and family conversations. What he found was striking—people consistently predicted they could shift opinions across a 40-point gap on a 100-point scale. When researchers then measured actual opinion change, the real movement was closer to 5 points. We're off by a factor of eight. That's not a rounding error. That's a fundamental misreading of human psychology.

The paradox cuts deeper. The people who were *most confident* in their ability to change minds were often the least effective at it. Grant calls this the "missionary problem." We develop an idea, become convinced of its truth, and then assume that others simply haven't encountered the right evidence yet. We become missionaries. And missionaries are notoriously bad at actually converting people—mostly because conversion requires understanding what the other person actually believes and why.

When you're a missionary, you stop being a student. You stop being curious about the gap between your view and theirs. You just become louder. Research on political polarization shows this clearly: the more someone reads arguments supporting their existing position, the more polarized they become. More certainty doesn't create more persuasion. It creates more entrenchment.

This matters because we live in a world that desperately needs us to change each other's minds. Not to abandon our convictions, but to actually move toward understanding. A parent convinced they know the right path forward with their teenager. A manager who sees the obvious solution that the team keeps missing. A friend worried about another friend's choices. We all hold the belief that we could convince the people we care about—if only they'd listen.

The evidence suggests something more humble: we probably can't.

But here's where it gets interesting. Grant's research also shows what *does* work. It's not better arguments. It's not more passion. It's questions. Specifically, it's what he calls "rethinking conversations"—where you genuinely don't know what the other person thinks or why, and you ask to understand. Not rhetorical questions. Not leading questions. Questions where you're actually uncertain of the answer.

This is harder than it sounds. When you ask someone why they believe what they believe, you have to genuinely want to know. If your real intent is to find the weakness in their logic so you can exploit it, they sense it immediately. Humans have a powerful detector for whether you're trying to understand them or trying to win against them.

The research on this is almost embarrassing in how clear it is. When people feel understood—truly heard and genuinely respected—they become *more* open to different perspectives, not less. When they feel like they're being lectured or tricked, they double down on their existing beliefs. This is true whether we're talking about someone's views on vaccines, education, religion, or how to spend a Saturday afternoon.

What does this look like in practice? A manager doesn't come to their team with a PowerPoint on why the current approach is wrong. They say, "I'm noticing some resistance to this new system. I might be missing something. Help me understand what concerns you about the change." And they actually listen. Not to find holes in the logic, but to understand what matters to the person who's resisting.

A parent doesn't lecture a teenager about life choices. They ask: "What are you hoping this will do for you? What are you worried might happen?" Then they shut up and listen. Really listen. This doesn't mean they abandon their perspective. It means they enter the conversation as a learner first.

The most persuasive people Grant studied weren't the ones with the best evidence. They were the ones who understood that changing a mind is always a collaborative process. You're not downloading information into someone else's brain. You're two people trying to navigate toward a more accurate picture of reality together. And that only works if the other person believes you're genuinely trying to understand their map of the world first.

This is why the hardest conversations often feel like they go nowhere in the moment. You ask questions instead of making arguments. You share your uncertainty instead of your convictions. You explicitly say the things you might be wrong about. It feels weak. It feels like you're losing. In the very short term, maybe you are. You're not dominating the conversation.

But something interesting happens in the hours and days after someone truly listens to you without judgment. You start to think differently. Not because they convinced you with a brilliant argument, but because you felt safe enough to actually rethink your position. The change comes from inside, not from outside. It sticks because it's yours.

This is the insight that reframes everything. If you want to be the kind of person who actually shifts how others think—not just in a moment, but in a lasting way—you have to stop trying so hard. You have to become genuinely curious about the gap between your map of reality and theirs. You have to ask questions you don't know the answer to. You have to be willing to discover that maybe, just maybe, you were wrong about something.

The irony? The people who accept this are far more likely to actually change other people's minds than the people who are certain they know how.

📌 **Takeaway**: In your next disagreement with someone whose mind you want to change, ask one genuine question about their perspective before sharing your own. Not as a tactic to lower their defenses, but as a real attempt to understand. Notice what happens next.

💡 **Extension**: Morgan Housel in *Same as Ever* echoes this—we're all running on incomplete information, and the humility to admit what we don't know is more powerful than doubling down on what we think we do. Arthur Brooks in *Love Your Enemies* makes a similar case: understanding the human beneath the disagreement transforms the possibility of connection.
